Philips · UX Telemetry Expert — Adoption & Insights · 579397

UX telemetry is the full delivery cycle: question, behaviour, tracking, dashboard, adoption.

The role sits inside the Usability Center of Excellence, not a standalone analytics function. The work is not only producing dashboards; it is helping product teams define the right questions, translate them into measurable behaviours, coordinate implementation, and make the resulting evidence usable inside product and UX decisions.

That is the overlap with my work: analytics architecture treated as a research instrument. I build the measurement layer from question framing through event definition, dashboarding, stakeholder enablement, and iteration — with Sense-Making Methodology behind the way questions are formulated.

Operating model

The telemetry cycle starts before the event exists.

The role brief describes end-to-end implementation: objectives, UX telemetry questions, data needs, tracking plans, implementation coordination, dashboarding, insight generation, adoption, and iteration. The six phases below are the operating shape I would bring to that work.

01 · Frame the question
Define the product, UX, or workflow decision the team needs telemetry to support. Sense-Making is the discipline here: surface the gap between what the system does, what the user needs, and what the team is trying to decide.
02 · Model the behaviour
Translate the question into observable actions: task outcomes, friction signals, omissions, recovery paths, and workarounds. The behaviour model is the bridge between qualitative question and event specification.
03 · Specify the tracking
Write event definitions, data-layer requirements, properties, consent constraints, and QA criteria so engineering can ship from them and the result can be audited against the original question.
04 · Coordinate delivery
Work across product, design, engineering, architecture, and analytics to get the telemetry built correctly. In enterprise analytics, the implementation work is often the adoption work beginning early.
05 · Build the view
Create dashboards anchored on the original question: task success, journey patterns, workflow performance, friction surfaces, and behaviour over time. The goal is not chart count; it is product decision support.
06 · Drive adoption
The system matters only if the team uses it. Structured handoff, dashboard walkthroughs, analyst enablement, executive readouts, and documentation are part of the telemetry cycle, not aftercare.

Case Studies

Five engagements, five parts of the telemetry cycle.

Each case maps to a responsibility in the role brief. Names are anonymised where engagements are under NDA.

Case 01 · Question formulation Consumer electronics retailer · 2023–2025

The workaround broke. The data showed the gap. Frontline staff knew why.

An anomaly inside a lifecycle build led to a Sense-Making-informed conversation with floor staff, surfacing a broken shadow IT tool the designed system had never supported. The client shipped a compatibility configurator that is now central to the business.

For the role · Product teams define better telemetry questions when behavioural data and frontline context meet. This is the question-framing layer before the tracking plan.

Case 02 · Instrumentation State-regulated retail platform · 2025–2026

From "what should we measure?" to 30+ shipped tickets.

A brand-new mobile app shipped with a full UX telemetry layer underneath it: 30+ tickets authored and deployed across iOS and Android, with cross-platform schema parity, GA4 e-commerce taxonomy, and TrustArc + OneTrust consent management built in before launch.

For the role · Translating questions into concrete event definitions, tracking plans, and implementation requirements under privacy and consent constraints.

Case 03 · AI workflow design Major quick-service restaurant brand · production

Designing AI workflows that do not outsource the thinking.

A production alert-triage system on Claude API + Amplitude with explicit gates around where AI assists and where human judgement is preserved. V1 ran on real on-call volume from 13–26 April 2026: 230 alerts became 39 posts, an 83% reduction, and one critical missed feature failure surfaced.

For the role · AI-supported analytical opportunities where the design question is not whether AI can summarise, but where human accountability remains in the workflow.

Case 04 · Adoption State-regulated retail platform · 2025–2026

The reporting layer survived the people who built it.

BigQuery, Dataform, and Looker Studio reporting infrastructure replaced recurring manual report pulls, paired with a three-session structured handoff so the client team could maintain and extend the system independently.

For the role · Data storytelling, data literacy, and evidence-based product management measured by adoption: 38 analyst hours recovered per month and zero data-infrastructure ramp-up after turnover.

Case 05 · Mixed-methods research methodology National home services franchise · 280+ locations · 2021–2022

Telemetry locates. Method diagnoses.

Quantitative GA4 telemetry paired with a UserZoom protocol built around SMM-informed neutral questioning, producing a sequential protocol: telemetry locates, method diagnoses, design responds.

For the role · The usability and human-factors bridge: quantitative telemetry paired with qualitative research so the team can diagnose the type of gap, not only the location of the drop-off.

Clinical Domain

No medical-device portfolio. Clear transfer from workflow telemetry.

The role asks for Healthtech context. This is the honest boundary and the transferable reasoning.

What is on this page — and what is not
What I bring: HIPAA exposure on protected data in a prior engagement, production consent-management work, Sense-Making Methodology with roots in health-information research, and the telemetry reasoning clinical workflows require: meaningful omissions, load-bearing workarounds, and friction that may not appear as simple failure.

What I do not bring: I have not worked inside a medical-device team and am not claiming hospital-floor experience or device-specific instrumentation history. SaMD-specific constraints, IEC 62366 / ISO 13485 / IEC 62304 / MDR compliance would be a learning curve supported by Philips colleagues who have lived them.

Standard consumer analytics assumptions do not transfer cleanly to clinical workflow. The instrumentation layer has to be designed around that difference.

Layer What it measures Instrumentation approach Clinical-specific constraint
Completion
Did the clinician reach the end state? Task finished, form submitted, record saved.
Standard event tracking. Task start / task end. Time-on-task per step.
Completion has clinical meaning, not just UX meaning. A completed task may still represent a risky workflow.
Omission
Was a designed step skipped? Not automatically an error: a deliberate or habitual bypass.
Bimodal time-to-completion distributions. Expected events that do not fire. Sequence-gap detection.
Omission may be rational, habitual, or a signal of design failure. It needs diagnosis before intervention.
Workaround
Did the clinician complete the task outside the designed system? Shadow process, external tool, verbal handoff.
Often cannot be instrumented directly. Surfaced through observation and neutral questioning.
Workarounds become load-bearing. When they break, the clinical impact appears — not always the workaround itself.
Passive collection priority
Instrument for what can be observed without adding cognitive load to the clinical task. Privacy, consent, and usability constraints narrow the instrumentation surface.
Omission is not failure
A skipped step is a research question before it is a bug. Diagnose whether the omission is intentional, habitual, or a signal of design failure.
Workaround detection
Shadow IT, verbal handoffs, and external tools are often the telemetry gap. The only way to find them is to pair telemetry with observation.
Methodological grounding
Sense-Making Methodology was developed by Brenda Dervin partly through health-information research at Ohio State. The study guide I published includes a working question rewriter — a small instrument for the “ask correctly” step that telemetry workshops depend on.

Why this role, why Philips.

On defining the right questions
This is the part of analytics I care about most: the moment before instrumentation, when the team decides what question the measurement layer is allowed to answer.
On AI-enabled telemetry use cases
The useful question is not whether AI can help. It is where AI assists, where humans remain accountable, and how the workflow prevents evidence from becoming unexamined automation.
On clinical workflow patterns and friction
I am not claiming a medical-device background. I am bringing workflow-telemetry reasoning, HIPAA exposure, consent implementation, and a methodology with health-information roots.
On adoption
Adoption means the team changes how it works. The dashboard is not the endpoint; the endpoint is a product team that can use the evidence without the original builder in the room.
