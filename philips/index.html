Philips · UX Telemetry Expert — Adoption & Insights · 579397

The job is the full telemetry cycle, not just the analytics outputs. That's exactly the shape of the work I do.

The role sits inside the Usability Center of Excellence, not a data team. The deliverable isn't a dashboard — it's helping product teams define the right questions, translate them into measurable behaviours, instrument them honestly, and adopt the measurement layer as a tool for better decisions. That is the operating model I've been running on enterprise engagements for over a decade.

What I bring that's less common: a working command of Sense-Making Methodology (Dervin) applied directly to analytics problems. That methodology is what makes question formulation a discipline rather than a guess — and it's how a measurement layer ends up surfacing what users can't or won't tell you directly.

Clinical Domain

No medical-device portfolio. Reasoning about clinical analytics that doesn't transfer from consumer.

Said plainly, then made useful.

What's on this page — and what isn't
What I bring: HIPAA exposure on protected data in a prior engagement — I've worked under the regime and understand the constraints in practice, not just from documentation. A Sense-Making methodology with roots in health-information research (Dervin's work was developed partly through health-information studies at Ohio State). And the analytical reasoning about what makes clinical instrumentation different from consumer product analytics — reasoning the JD asks for explicitly.

What I don't bring: I have not worked inside a medical-device team and am not claiming hospital-floor experience or device-specific instrumentation history. SaMD-specific constraints, IEC 62366 / ISO 13485 / IEC 62304 / MDR compliance — I would learn these on the job, supported by colleagues who have lived them.

Standard consumer analytics assumptions break down in clinical workflow. The instrumentation layer has to be designed around that, not retrofitted.

Layer What it measures Instrumentation approach Clinical-specific constraint
Completion
Did the clinician reach the end state? Task finished, form submitted, record saved.
Standard event tracking. Task start / task end. Time-on-task per step.
Completion has clinical meaning, not just UX meaning. A completed task may still represent a dangerous workflow.
Omission
Was a designed step skipped? Not an error — a deliberate or habitual bypass.
Bimodal time-to-completion distributions. Expected events that don't fire. Sequence-gap detection.
Omission may be rational (the step adds no clinical value) or a workaround signal. Must distinguish before intervening.
Workaround
Did the clinician complete the task outside the designed system? Shadow process, external tool, verbal handoff.
Cannot be instrumented directly. Surfaced through observational research and SMM neutral questioning.
Workarounds become load-bearing. When they break, the clinical impact appears — not the workaround itself.
Passive collection priority
Instrument for what can be observed without adding cognitive load to the clinical task. IEC62366 and consent constraints narrow the instrumentation surface — design for what's within it, not what would be ideal.
Omission is not failure
A skipped step is a research question, not a bug. Diagnose before intervening. SMM-structured questions surface whether the omission is intentional, habitual, or a signal of design failure.
Workaround detection
Shadow IT, verbal handoffs, and external tools are the telemetry gap. The only way to find them is to go where the work is done. Observation, not dashboard review.
Methodological grounding
Sense-Making Methodology was developed by Brenda Dervin partly through health-information research at Ohio State. The study guide I published includes a working question rewriter (Hugging Face Inference Providers via a small Vercel API) — an instrument for the exact "ask correctly" step that clinical interview design depends on.

If the role were mine — first 90 days, a provisional sketch
Days 1–30 · Listen
Sit with each Healthtech product team currently working with the CoE. Run structured Sense-Making interviews on what the measurement layer is and isn't doing for them, and what decisions they're trying to make. The first 30 days produces no telemetry — just a map of the questions.
Days 31–60 · Map
Audit the existing tracking-plan governance and dashboard estate across the portfolio. Identify the highest-leverage cross-product gaps: where the same user action is named differently, where omission is being read as failure, where workarounds have become load-bearing. Propose, don't impose.
Days 61–90 · Land one
Pick one product team and one question and deliver a full telemetry-cycle pass — question to spec to instrumentation to dashboard to enablement — as the working pattern for everything that follows. Build the muscle by exercising it, not by writing more documentation.
