Case study · for the Philips brief · Topic 07 · Data Engineering & Architecture

Three raw scans → one. Fifteen CTEs → zero. ~50% cost reduction.

An audit and architectural redesign of a GA4 → BigQuery pipeline that had grown organically into a 1.2 TB intermediate table and a $112/run cascading CTE chain.

/ State-regulated retail platform · analytics architect · April 2026

Why this is in the Philips selection
The systems-thinking that scales to large telemetry estates. Pipeline debt accumulates the same way technical debt does — one reasonable decision at a time, each solving an immediate problem without visibility into the cumulative architecture. The optimisation here is the discipline of stepping back to look at the whole system, identifying where costs are actually coming from, and redesigning around a single shared computation rather than repeated independent ones. Same discipline applies to any telemetry estate at Philips scale — cost-thinking that respects both the analytics needs and the infrastructure budget.
→
What happened
A GA4 → BigQuery analytics pipeline that had grown organically into a ~$112/run CTE chain processing ~18 TB per execution was redesigned around a single shared computation: ~50% cost reduction across pipeline and dashboard queries, the 1.2 TB GA4_Event_Params intermediate table retired, heavy dashboards halved from ~$200 to ~$100/day. Delivered as a 4-phase migration plan with a 2–4 week validation window. The optimisation was architectural, not query-level.
Pipeline debt accumulates the same way technical debt does — one reasonable decision at a time, each solving an immediate problem without visibility into the cumulative architecture. The 1.2 TB GA4_Event_Params table was a reasonable intermediate step when first introduced. The 15-CTE PDP extraction was a reasonable way to get the data. The problem was that no one had stepped back to look at the whole system.
Situation
A state-regulated retail platform's GA4 → BigQuery analytics pipeline, grown organically over several engagements: events_* scanned three separate times (200–400 GB each at $6.25/TB); Step 02.25 unnested every event type into a generic key-value table (GA4_Event_Params — 1.2 TB, 4.67 billion rows); Step 02.5 ran 15+ separate CTEs each independently scanning that 1.2 TB table and FULL OUTER JOIN-ed them together. Total: ~18 TB processed per run, ~$112/run. On top of that, SELECT DISTINCT on 30–50 columns in three steps, duplicated and slightly inconsistent channel-grouping CASE logic, and a 429 GB unpartitioned table forcing full scans on every downstream query.
Task
Audit the whole pipeline, find where the cost was actually coming from, and redesign — not optimise queries in place. The brief was implicit: surface architectural waste, then propose a migration plan the team could execute against without a freeze. Cost reduction was the visible target; maintainability and consistency were the real ones.
Action
Single raw event scan via conditional aggregation — one GROUP BY with MAX(IF(ep.key = 'x', ep.value.string_value, NULL)) for each of 25+ event parameter keys simultaneously, producing a flat analysis-ready row per page_view event with all parameters pivoted into columns. 8-step sequential pipeline restructured as a 6-step DAG with three tiers of parallel execution. SELECT DISTINCT replaced with ROW_NUMBER dedup and GROUP BY. Channel grouping unified into a single canonical CASE definition. Partitioning added to the largest reporting tables, converting full-table scans into pruned daily reads. Migration plan delivered as a 4-phase rollout: schema preparation, historical backfill, scheduled-query deployment, table cleanup — with a 2–4 week validation window.
Result
The optimisation wasn't about writing faster queries — it was about understanding the full data flow, identifying where costs were actually coming from, and redesigning around a single shared computation rather than repeated independent ones. The pipeline ended cheaper, faster, easier to maintain, and more consistent in its output: channel-grouping logic now defined in one place instead of three, two intermediate tables retired without information loss. The decision pattern transfers to any cost-conscious analytics rebuild — spend the modelling work once.
