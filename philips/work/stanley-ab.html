Case study · for the Philips brief · Topic 03 · Culture change

Introducing A/B testing as a leadership practice.

Five years building marketing analytics from scratch at a 280-location franchise — including the single-customer / householding data model and the experimentation culture that came with it.

/ Stanley Steemer · 280 franchise locations · five years

Why this is in the Philips selection
The adoption story behind shifting an organisation toward measurement-first decisions. Five years of building measurement practice as a leadership-decision tool, not a marketing tactic. The same long-arc work the Adoption & Insights division does internally at Philips — introducing analytics as the substrate for decisions across operational, product, and commercial teams.
→
What happened
A 280-franchise-location home services brand built its marketing analytics function from scratch on an SSMS / Azure SQL Server foundation — including the single-customer / householding data model that gave the business its first reliable identity resolution across the multi-location estate. Introduced A/B testing as a leadership decision-making practice, not a marketing tactic. By year two, leadership was actively requesting data-informed decisions; the culture had shifted from intuition-first to test-first. The original A/B framework was still in use when I left.
Context
Joined Stanley Steemer with no experimentation culture, no marketing analytics function, and an ageing BI stack on the way out. 280 franchise locations with no reliable identity resolution across the estate — which meant no marketing automation, no CRM strategy, and no shared definition of "customer" at the business level. The work that needed to happen wasn't a tool migration; it was a cultural and architectural rebuild.
What I did
Built the marketing analytics function over five years on an Azure SQL Server foundation (SSMS for development, SSRS for reporting — even on GA4 data). Wrote the Python ingestion layer that pulled Universal Analytics into the warehouse, and later wired the GA4 → BigQuery export back into Azure via a server-side connection — all analytical data lived in the warehouse, not in the source platforms. Designed the single-customer concept and the householding data model that gave the business its first identity resolution across 280 locations, enabling marketing automation and CRM strategy where neither existed. Introduced A/B testing as a leadership tool — framed as decision-support, not as a marketing tactic — and stewarded the practice through year-two adoption. The framework survived rolloff.
