Topic 01

Question Formulation & Discovery

Before any instrumentation, the right question. The two cases below show what Sense-Making questioning and structured discovery surface that data alone cannot.

For: UX Telemetry · Insight · Product Strategy

Case / Content brand · freelance · 2022–2024

When the homepage isn't the front door.

A discovery process that surfaced six tracking gaps the client didn't know existed — and one finding that reframed the editorial strategy.

A high-profile content brand — podcasts, newsletter, events, books — had Google Analytics installed and could see traffic numbers. They could not answer the questions that drove their business: were people actually listening to episodes, what drove newsletter signups, which pages were genuinely performing. They had data. They did not have answers.
01
Structured discovery
Documented Q&A with agency and client: what the data was and wasn't doing, what decisions it should be informing, what they thought was stopping conversions.
02
Audit against the questions
Six technical gaps surfaced: double-firing pageviews, broken UTMs, no conversion tracking, podcast engagement entirely unmeasured, event taxonomy misaligned, iframe player events firing incorrectly.
03
Rebuild around real goals
Full GA4 implementation with scroll depth, episode engagement, 2-min engaged-session conversion, newsletter signups, content-level custom dimensions, source/medium fixed.
04
Build for adoption
Loom training video library (custom dimensions, conversions, explorations, entrances/exits, real-time) so the team could operate the implementation without the analyst.
The assumption
The homepage was the primary entry point. Audience experience was centred on it. Editorial decisions and optimisation effort followed from that premise.
What behaviour showed
Once the instrumentation was honest, podcast episode pages were where the most engaged users were spending time — entering directly, staying longer, taking more actions than homepage visitors. The homepage-first model didn't match the audience that actually existed.
→
Outcome
The agency returned in October 2022 with substantive usage questions — signal that the implementation was being used, not shelved. Two years later, in April 2024, the same agency came back unprompted with a GTM issue. The relationship was still live. That's the only adoption metric that matters.

Case / Consumer electronics retailer · lifecycle marketing · 2023–2025

A workaround broke. The transaction data confessed.

An anomaly surfaced inside an unrelated lifecycle model — and led to a product capability that's now central to the business.

Most "interesting" findings in analytics arrive in the middle of doing something else. The data doesn't tell you where to look — observation does. The role of instrumentation is to make the gap between designed system and lived practice visible enough that a question becomes worth asking.
The quantitative signal
Building customer lifecycle segments for post-purchase marketing. An odd pattern: a meaningful cohort returning shortly after purchase to buy additional components. Nobody asked for the investigation. The anomaly surfaced while building something else.
What observation found
A shadow IT tool — built informally by staff to guide customers through part-compatibility questions — had stopped working. The designed system never supported compatibility guidance. When the workaround broke, the gap became visible in transaction behaviour.
Sense-Making applied
Neutral questioning with floor staff — structured to let the diagnosis emerge from the people experiencing the friction. The finding: staff knew exactly what was broken and what they needed. The analyst's job was to ask correctly, not to diagnose independently.
Influence without authority
Presented to the CX team across stakeholders without formal authority over product. The compatibility-guidance gap was resourced and addressed — eventually becoming a core product capability the business now organises around.
→
Outcome
The client subsequently built and deployed a full compatibility-checking configurator — now central to the business, with automatic part filtering, inventory integration, and a dedicated assembly service tier built around it. Root cause confirmed by the product that shipped.

The time I instrumented the wrong question.

Draft — replace with the actual engagement. The brief asked for funnel instrumentation on a multi-step checkout. I built the funnel cleanly — structured taxonomy, GA events, stakeholder-friendly dashboards — and shipped it. Three months in, conversion rates were stable but revenue was sliding. Observation eventually showed the loss was upstream: returning customers were abandoning before they ever reached the funnel, because of a category-page change nobody had instrumented for. The funnel I'd built was answering the wrong question — "how do users complete checkout?" — when the question that actually mattered was "what changed about why people came back at all?"

Compact · SDK debugging Major quick-service restaurant · Segment · analytics-swift 1.9.1

A silent iOS data-loss bug, traced to the initialisation sequence.

iOS was systematically undercounting Application Opened events compared to Android. Investigation traced the root cause to the app's initialisation sequence: rawSegment.enabled = false at startup, re-enabled only after remote config completed. The Segment SDK 1.9.1 process(incomingEvent:) returns immediately when enabled == false — cold-launch events fired before the gate flipped were silently dropped, not queued. Same engagement also surfaced cross-platform tracking-plan parity gaps (web on legacy dataLayer/GA naming; apps on a unified Title Case Segment model) and a GTM misconfiguration where two international locales were feeding the main analytics container.

Compact · Error taxonomy Major quick-service restaurant · Amplitude monitors

Clean taxonomy, trustworthy alerts, enriched context.

Three layers of remediation on an alert channel where genuine checkout failures were buried in noise from low-traffic Active% triggers. Layer 1 redesigned alert thresholds (absolute rate + rate-of-change) and added engineering-facing troubleshooting guidance directly on each chart. Layer 2 designed a hierarchical, stable error-category ID scheme (SYS-INF-001, SYS-APP-VAL-010) with deterministic mapping rules and governance guidelines to prevent schema drift. Layer 3 added cart_id to Checkout Started Error Received events so engineering could correlate specific basket instances to checkout failures — error events must carry the same context as their success counterparts.

The taxonomy work directly reduces false positives in the AI alert triage system (see Topic 04).

Topic 03

Experimentation & Release Measurement

Designing the measurement framework before the change ships, so a product decision can be evaluated as a controlled experiment rather than a post-hoc story.

For: Product Analytics · Marketing Analytics · UX Telemetry

Case / Major quick-service restaurant · analytics architect · navigation redesign

The measurement plan written before the code.

Proposed and owned the release measurement and A/B test framework for a major mobile-nav redesign — turning a planned straight-cutover into a controlled experiment.

UX changes of this scale are impossible to evaluate fairly without a pre-defined measurement plan: baselines haven't been captured, the right events aren't instrumented, and success criteria get defined retroactively to match whatever the data shows. The argument for an A/B test is also an argument for not being fooled by your own product launch.
Deliverable 01 — Event tagging requirements
Defined the full analytics requirements before development began: new events for tab taps, nav state changes, back-navigation patterns; required properties tab_name, previous_tab, platform; audit of existing screen_view coverage against the new nav destinations. Engineering-ready Jira story with acceptance criteria tied to dev tickets.
Deliverable 02 — Release tracking plan + A/B rationale
A written tracking plan covering metrics to monitor before, during, and after launch, plus an A/B-test-rationale one-pager for the internal team and client stakeholders — explaining why a controlled release isolates the nav change as the causal variable and enables rollback with statistical confidence.
What got measured
Primary: Transactions (North Star) impact. Funnel: screen_view → add_to_cart → order_placed conversion at each step. Nav-specific: tab tap distribution, time-to-first-order, deals/loyalty discovery rate. Guardrails: session length, crash rate, error rate.
Confounding addressed explicitly
The A/B rationale called out the risk of confounding with other in-flight changes (e.g., an Upsells v1.0 release running concurrently) — a key argument for controlled release over straight cutover. Statistical significance threshold and minimum run duration defined up front.
→
Where the engagement ended
Specs, tracking plan, and A/B-rationale one-pager delivered to engineering and the client analytics lead; test ownership and rollout decision held by the client team. The architectural deliverable was the measurement framework that turned a planned straight cutover into a controlled experiment — the post-launch result and rollout decision sit under client confidentiality.

Topic 04

AI Workflow Design

The interesting work in AI-assisted analytics isn't the model — it's the gates. Where AI assists, where human judgement is preserved, and why those lines are where they are.

For: Product Analytics · UX Telemetry · AI workflow design

Case / Major quick-service restaurant brand · analytics architect

Designing AI workflows that don't outsource the thinking.

A production alert-triage system with explicit decisions about where AI assists and where human judgement is preserved — and why those lines are where they are.

The risk in AI-assisted analytics isn't automation — it's outsourced thinking. A workflow that delegates diagnosis to a model removes the human judgement step that makes triage meaningful. This system is documented around explicit gates: surface, suppress, escalate when warranted, never silence a worsening issue with its own earlier post. V1 ran in production from 13–26 April 2026 on real on-call volume; currently offline pending V2 build.
01
Suppress known issues
Reads the alert channel. Records monitors already posted today including peak values. Silences for the rest of the day unless escalated significantly.
02
Pull, dedupe, pre-screen
Last ~65 minutes from production and staging alert channels. Groups firings by monitor name. Drops without querying: overnight 0→near-zero spikes and misconfigured "0.0 higher than 0.0" monitors.
03
Trend + error breakdown
Queries Amplitude for each surviving monitor's 7-day chart. Surfaces error-type breakdown. Known low-signal errors auto-downgraded.
04
Gate, group, post
Critical or Warning only. Suppressed monitor hitting 2× prior value overrides suppression. Co-firing monitors grouped under one root cause. Output: one structured message, plain-English summary, next steps.
Where human judgement is preserved
The system surfaces and ranks — it does not diagnose or decide. The plain-English summary exists because a previous critical checkout regression went unactioned because technical percentages obscured the signal. The human still acts. The AI makes the signal readable.
What cognitive surrender would look like
Auto-resolve, auto-assign, auto-deprioritise without a human gate. The escalation override exists specifically to prevent the suppression logic from becoming a way to silence a worsening issue with its own earlier post.

Full Oracle Responsys → Bloomreach CDP migration — customer data model, event schemas, segmentation, and triggered-campaign architecture all redesigned. Zero campaign downtime through cutover.

Marketing-platform migrations fail two ways: by breaking campaign continuity (data gaps that don't close cleanly post-migration), or by carrying the previous platform's data model forward unchanged (winning the migration, losing the architectural opportunity). Customer data model
Redesigned from scratch for Bloomreach's CDP primitives — not lifted from the Responsys schema. Customer identity stitched cleanly across email, SMS, and on-site behavioural events, with consent state carried through the model rather than bolted on after.
Event schemas & segmentation
Lifecycle event taxonomy authored from product, transactional, and engagement signals. Segmentation logic rewritten to be queryable rather than enumerated — cohorts defined by behaviour rather than by static list membership.
Triggered campaign architecture
Triggered-campaign automation built on the new lifecycle event tracking — the behavioural signal the segmentation logic queries against. GDPR-compliant consent management maintained through cutover via the same configuration regime.

Topic 07

Data Engineering & Architecture

Pipeline architecture, cost-thinking, schema design, and the patient work of stepping back to look at the whole system — not just writing faster queries.

For: Data Engineer · Analytics Engineer · Architect

Case / State-regulated retail platform · analytics architect · April 2026

Three raw scans → one. Fifteen CTEs → zero. ~50% cost reduction.

An audit and architectural redesign of a GA4 → BigQuery pipeline that had grown organically into a 1.2 TB intermediate table and a $112/run cascading CTE chain.

Pipeline debt accumulates the same way technical debt does — one reasonable decision at a time, each solving an immediate problem without visibility into the cumulative architecture. The 1.2 TB GA4_Event_Params table was a reasonable intermediate step when first introduced. The 15-CTE PDP extraction was a reasonable way to get the data. The problem was that no one had stepped back to look at the whole system.
Where the money was going
The original pipeline scanned events_* three separate times (200–400 GB each at $6.25/TB). Step 02.25 unnested every event type into a generic key-value table (GA4_Event_Params — 1.2 TB, 4.67 billion rows). Step 02.5 then ran 15+ separate CTEs each independently scanning that full 1.2 TB table and FULL OUTER JOIN-ed them together: ~18 TB processed per run, the primary cost driver at ~$112/run. On top of that: SELECT DISTINCT on 30–50 columns in three steps; channel-grouping CASE logic duplicated and slightly inconsistent across steps; a 429 GB unpartitioned table forcing full scans on every downstream query.
What replaced it
Single raw event scan via conditional aggregation — one GROUP BY with MAX(IF(ep.key = 'x', ep.value.string_value, NULL)) for each of 25+ event parameter keys simultaneously, producing a flat analysis-ready row per page_view event with all parameters pivoted into columns. 8-step sequential pipeline restructured as a 6-step DAG with three tiers of parallel execution. SELECT DISTINCT replaced with ROW_NUMBER dedup and GROUP BY. Channel grouping unified into a single canonical CASE definition. Partitioning added to the largest reporting tables, converting full-table scans into pruned daily reads.
