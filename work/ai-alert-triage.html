Case study · Topic 04 · AI Workflow Design

Designing AI workflows that don't outsource the thinking.

A production alert-triage system with explicit decisions about where AI assists and where human judgement is preserved — and why those lines are where they are.

/ Major quick-service restaurant brand · analytics architect

→
What happened
A production AI alert-triage system compressed 230 raw alerts to 39 posts (83% reduction) across a two-week run on real on-call volume (13–26 April 2026) — and surfaced one critical feature failure the human-only review had missed. That signal would have continued silently otherwise. The architecture: AI surfaces and ranks; the human still diagnoses and acts. V2 build in progress.
The risk in AI-assisted analytics isn't automation — it's outsourced thinking. A workflow that delegates diagnosis to a model removes the human judgement step that makes triage meaningful. This system is documented around explicit gates: surface, suppress, escalate when warranted, never silence a worsening issue with its own earlier post.
Situation
A noisy alert channel at a major QSR's analytics stack: roughly 230 alerts per fortnight from production and staging monitors, with a known failure mode — a prior critical checkout regression had gone unactioned because technical percentages obscured the signal. The on-call team needed the signal to be readable, not just present.
Task
Design an AI-assisted triage pipeline that compresses the noise without removing the human gate. The brief: surface what matters, suppress what doesn't, escalate when a suppressed signal worsens — and never delegate the diagnose-and-act decision to a model.
Action
A four-step pipeline (detail in the flow below) running hourly: suppress known issues, pull / dedupe / pre-screen, fetch 7-day trend + error breakdown from Amplitude, gate · group · post. Two explicit gates around the AI: the system surfaces and ranks — it does not diagnose or decide. The plain-English summary exists because the previous critical checkout regression went unactioned when technical percentages obscured the signal. The escalation override exists specifically to prevent suppression logic from silencing a worsening issue with its own earlier post.
Result
V1 ran 13–26 April 2026 on real on-call volume. 230 raw alerts compressed to 39 AI-posted findings (83% reduction). Hourly alert-to-action cycle versus the previous multi-hour manual review. Most consequentially: the AI surfaced one critical feature failure the on-call team had missed in their human-only review — the signal that justifies the design. V2 build in progress.
01
Suppress known issues
Reads the alert channel. Records monitors already posted today including peak values. Silences for the rest of the day unless escalated significantly.
02
Pull, dedupe, pre-screen
Last ~65 minutes from production and staging alert channels. Groups firings by monitor name. Drops without querying: overnight 0→near-zero spikes and misconfigured "0.0 higher than 0.0" monitors.
03
Trend + error breakdown
Queries Amplitude for each surviving monitor's 7-day chart. Surfaces error-type breakdown. Known low-signal errors auto-downgraded.
04
Gate, group, post
Critical or Warning only. Suppressed monitor hitting 2× prior value overrides suppression. Co-firing monitors grouped under one root cause. Output: one structured message, plain-English summary, next steps.
