Case study · Lens 03 · Experimentation

The measurement plan written before the code.

Proposed and owned the release measurement and A/B test framework for a major mobile-nav redesign — turning a planned straight-cutover into a controlled experiment.

/ Major quick-service restaurant · analytics architect · navigation redesign

→
What happened
A major QSR's planned straight-cutover nav redesign was reshaped into a controlled A/B release by proposing and owning the measurement framework before development began — event tagging spec, written tracking plan, and an A/B-rationale one-pager that isolated the nav change as the causal variable. The architectural deliverable was the framework itself; specs, tracking plan, and rationale handed to engineering and the client analytics lead, with test ownership and rollout decision held by the client team. Post-launch results sit under client confidentiality.
UX changes of this scale are impossible to evaluate fairly without a pre-defined measurement plan: baselines haven't been captured, the right events aren't instrumented, and success criteria get defined retroactively to match whatever the data shows. The argument for an A/B test is also an argument for not being fooled by your own product launch.
Situation
A major quick-service restaurant brand's mobile app heading into a significant navigation redesign — planned as a straight cutover with no controlled-release framing. Baselines hadn't been captured, the right events weren't instrumented for the new IA, and there was no contract for what would count as success or failure. An Upsells v1.0 release was running concurrently, raising the risk of confounding any post-launch reading.
Task
Propose and own the release measurement framework that turned the planned cutover into a controlled experiment — written before code. Three things had to be true: the right events had to be specced and instrumented before the redesign shipped; metrics, guardrails, and success criteria had to be agreed up front; and the confounding risk from concurrent releases had to be called out explicitly in the rationale.
Action
Two written deliverables. Event tagging requirements: new events for tab taps, nav state changes, back-navigation patterns; required properties tab_name, previous_tab, platform; an audit of existing screen_view coverage against the new nav destinations — engineering-ready Jira story with acceptance criteria tied to dev tickets. Release tracking plan + A/B-rationale one-pager: metrics to monitor before, during, and after launch (Primary: Transactions / North Star; Funnel: screen_view → add_to_cart → order_placed; Nav-specific: tap distribution, time-to-first-order, deals/loyalty discovery; Guardrails: session length, crash rate, error rate). The rationale called out the Upsells v1.0 confounding risk and defined statistical significance threshold and minimum run duration up front.
Result
Specs, tracking plan, and A/B-rationale one-pager delivered to engineering and the client analytics lead. The planned straight-cutover became a controlled release with rollback options and statistical confidence. Test ownership and rollout decision held by the client team; the architectural deliverable was the measurement framework itself, not the result of any single experiment. Post-launch readings and the eventual rollout decision sit under client confidentiality.
