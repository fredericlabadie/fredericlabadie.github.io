Case study · Topic 01 · Question Formulation

A workaround broke. The transaction data confessed.

An anomaly surfaced inside an unrelated lifecycle model — and led to a product capability that's now central to the business.

/ Consumer electronics retailer · lifecycle marketing · 2023–2025

→
What happened
A consumer-electronics retailer now organises around a compatibility-checking configurator — automatic part filtering, inventory integration, a dedicated assembly service tier, mobile-first for floor use, with a second iteration underway shaped by ongoing staff input. The capability did not start as a feature request. It started inside an unrelated lifecycle-marketing build, with a transactional anomaly nobody had asked about, and a Sense-Making conversation with floor staff that surfaced what the designed system had never supported.
Most "interesting" findings in analytics arrive in the middle of doing something else. The data doesn't tell you where to look — observation does. The role of instrumentation is to make the gap between designed system and lived practice visible enough that a question becomes worth asking.
Situation
Consumer-electronics retailer, lifecycle marketing engagement, 2023–2025. Building customer lifecycle segments for post-purchase marketing. An odd pattern emerged: a meaningful cohort returning shortly after purchase to buy additional components. Nobody had asked for the investigation; the anomaly surfaced while building something else.
Task
Turn the quantitative anomaly into a diagnosed root cause without overstepping the analyst's remit. The funnel showed where the gap was — it did not say why. Closing that gap meant surfacing the diagnosis from the people who held it (floor staff) and routing it to the team that could act on it (CX / Product), without formal authority over either.
Action
Two-part move. First, Sense-Making–informed neutral questioning with floor staff — structured to let the diagnosis emerge from the people experiencing the friction, not imposed by the analyst. The finding: a shadow IT tool, built informally by staff because the official tools were wrong or slow and had not been designed for floor use, had stopped working. The designed system had never supported compatibility guidance at all. After the initial anomaly surfaced, the finding was evaluated further with staff directly, and it became a focus worthy of organisational energy. Second, presented to the CX team across stakeholders without formal authority over product — framing the gap as a resourcing question rather than a recommendation.
Result
The compatibility-guidance gap was resourced and addressed. The client subsequently built and deployed a full compatibility-checking configurator — now central to the business, with automatic part filtering, inventory integration, and a dedicated assembly service tier built around it. Critically, the redesign was mobile-first: staff use it on the floor, in context, which had not been a design consideration before. Improved error messaging addressed a second friction point the staff evaluation surfaced. A second iteration is underway — a more focused, utilitarian tool shaped by continued staff input. Root cause confirmed by the product that shipped.
