# WHAT THE SKIN REMEMBERS
## Story Outline — Working Draft

---

## PART ONE: THE SENDING

### Chapter 1 — The Archive
*Open in the longhouse of the Recorder tribe.*

We meet the Recorder — formerly Tomis Franklin, now nameless, his entire body the archive. He moves through the longhouse the way very old things move: with economy, with weight. The Scribe — formerly Filamina — is at work on a new entry, her hands doing something extraordinary with needle and skin. Around them, the preserved skins of their predecessors hang in order, the oldest reaching back to firsthand accounts of the Collapse.

Naum is here because he has always been here. He knows the archive the way he knows his own hands — by feel, by repetition, by a hundred evenings of listening. He is nearly eighteen and leaving tomorrow and he is certain he knows what a true story looks like.

The Recorder calls him close and sends him out with no instructions except the ones Naum already has: *bring back something true.* The Scribe watches. She does not say goodbye. She is waiting for something, and it is not his return — it is what he brings back with him.

Naum leaves wearing his best.

---

### Chapter 2 — The Farm
*Naum enters the post-Collapse world.*

The Anabasis feeling begins immediately. An overpass consumed by forest, its concrete legs still standing in the tree line like ruins of a temple no one built for worship. A valley where something enormous once was — the footings still visible, the purpose illegible. Naum moves through this without grief. It is simply the world.

He finds Kess on a failing farm at the edge of the high country. The family is recently dead — the farm tells the story before she does. She is sitting on the porch, barefoot, a gourd banjo across her knees, and she is not crying. She is waiting, in the way of someone who doesn't yet know what they're waiting for.

Naum offers her a place in his traveling party before he has fully thought through what this means. She accepts before he finishes the sentence.

They set out. He is carrying a story about what kind of journey this is. She already knows it's a different one.

---

### Chapter 3 — The Cat Adopts a Family
*Tace joins.*

Two days out. The reader has been seeing things at the edges of scenes that Naum hasn't registered but Kess has — a pause at a crossroads, something small left at the base of a post, a brief attentiveness toward the treeline that she doesn't explain. Naum has been filing it under *folk practice* and moving on.

Then Tace steps out of the trees in front of them.

She introduces herself with the minimum number of words and begins walking with them. No ask. No quest hook. Naum opens his mouth to say something and Tace has already moved past him to look at a track in the mud.

That evening, Naum watches Kess perform her crossroads ritual and realizes Tace has been watching too — not with skepticism, not with deference, but with the specific quality of attention of someone building a new entry in a catalogue they've been keeping for years. This is the moment Tace decided, though neither of them knows that yet.

---

## PART TWO: THE JOURNEY

### Chapter 4 — The High Country
*Three become a party. The world gets stranger.*

The landscape shifts as they climb. Ruins at altitude — a fire tower rusted to sculpture, a ski resort whose lifts still hang in the trees like the skeleton of something that died mid-reach. The documents they find in an abandoned ranger station are perfectly legible and almost entirely uninterpretable. Naum photographs nothing in his mind because it requires no interpretation — this is just the world above a certain elevation.

Kess begins to feel something at night that she doesn't have a name for yet. Not lies. Lies are a pressure in her chest, clean and specific. This is different — a low-grade wrongness, like a frequency just below hearing. She doesn't mention it.

Tace crouches over everything. She narrates under her breath. She forgets to eat.

Naum is looking for his story and has not found it yet. He is beginning to wonder if the high country is the wrong direction.

---

### Chapter 5 — Marn
*The fourth arrives. The party's purpose shifts.*

They encounter Marn on a shared trail — or he encounters them, which is a different thing. He has a destination and a mission and a structured institutional coat and round glasses and the particular bearing of someone who has never been seriously wrong about anything and has organized his entire identity around this fact.

His mission: retrieve a document from a sealed federal installation in the high country that will confirm his tribe's founding narrative. He has the True Keeper's directions to the location. The directions are slightly wrong in ways he has already explained to himself.

He was sent out fully equipped. The True Keeper calculated what he would need — supplies, routes, contingencies, protocols for every foreseeable situation. The calculation was meticulous. The list was comprehensive. Most of it is exactly right for the journey the machine could model: the medical kit, the food ratios, the waterproofing. Some of it is calibrated for a world that no longer exists quite the way the machine believes it does. Marn has explanations for the gaps. He is good at explanations.

He and Naum do not trust each other. The Recorder tribe and the True Keeper tribe have a history of mutual condescension running in both directions. They have the same destination. They travel together.

Kess says nothing about Marn at first. She is listening to something the others can't hear.

---

### Chapter 6 — Fractures
*Small things crack. Nobody says so.*

The four travel. Marn's navigation is off by a margin he keeps adjusting. He has an explanation for each discrepancy. The explanations are getting harder to believe, even to him.

Tace asks him a question about a plant he identified incorrectly, in the way of someone genuinely curious about where the error came from. It lands badly. They spend two days in productive hostility before realizing they are both empiricists running on incompatible data sets.

A Not Deer encounter in the pre-dawn: Marn has a procedure. The procedure is wrong. Kess does what she does — places something, speaks something under her breath, turns her back to the tree line in a way that communicates without communicating. The thing loses interest and goes. Marn puts it in his journal under a heading he doesn't close.

His private explanation for all of it: external variables. The True Keeper gave him an accurate model; Naum and Kess are inputs the machine couldn't have anticipated. The equation was right. The variables changed. This is not the same as the machine being wrong. He believes this. He keeps believing it past the point where he finds it easy to believe.

Tace writes: *The child is not adjacent to the Wyrd. She is of it. Require further observation.*

Naum, watching all of this, has begun to suspect that the story he came out to find is not the story that is happening to him.

---

### Chapter 7 — The Pivot
*Naum understands he's been wrong about what kind of story this is.*

A night scene. Fire, downtime, everyone quiet in their own way. Naum has been quietly constructing a narrative in which the glory is helping Marn complete his mission — the tribesman who guides the scholar to the lost knowledge, who makes the retrieval possible. A good story. A story about doing something.

Something said or unsaid around the fire dismantles it. Maybe Kess simply turns to look at him at the wrong moment, and her expression is the expression of someone who has known for several days that he's been wrong about something.

He doesn't say anything. He goes quiet in the way he does when something has genuinely landed. Tace notes the silence.

He doesn't know yet what the true story is. But he stops expecting it to be the one he planned.

---

## PART THREE: THE BUNKER

### Chapter 8 — The Installation
*They arrive. The Anabasis feeling is fullest here.*

The federal installation is larger than any of them imagined. Pre-Collapse concrete and steel, a scale that reads as geological rather than human-made. Solar arrays still functional, which means the lights inside still work, which is somehow more disorienting than darkness would be. The four of them stand at the entrance and feel the weight of a civilization they never knew pressing through the walls.

Inside: everything is pristine. Vacuum-sealed. Documents in labeled filing systems, organized by a logic that is internally consistent and externally opaque. The smell of preserved paper. Fluorescent light. A world that ended here and stayed ended, perfectly.

Marn moves through it with the trembling focus of someone receiving what they were promised. Tace moves through it like a field site. Naum moves through it like a man who has just realized he is inside the most important story he will ever carry, and he doesn't know yet what the story is.

Kess stands in the entrance for a long moment before following. Her chest is full.

---

### Chapter 9 — What the Archive Kept
*Two discoveries. One breaks everything.*

Marn finds the document. It is exactly where the True Keeper said it would be. He opens it with hands that are not quite steady.

The document contains what he was sent to retrieve — and something else. A gap. A section of the founding narrative that doesn't match the machine's records because the machine, five generations ago, confabulated it. The truth and the error are both in the document, and the document is older than the machine's account of it. The machine invented the part it didn't know.

Across the room, Naum has found something else entirely: a document he recognizes. Not its content — its shape. He has heard the beginning of this story in the longhouse since he was a child. This is the second half. The record of what happened *after* — the consequences that were never recorded because the person who lived them never came home.

He holds it and understands, for the first time, what a true story actually is. Events without their consequences are incomplete truths. Someone had to walk far enough to find the other half.

---

### Chapter 10 — It's Been Guessing
*The moment the story turns on.*

Kess crosses the room to where Marn is standing. She doesn't say anything for a moment. Then she takes his hand.

*"It's been guessing. For a long time."*

He goes quiet. Not the quiet of gathering himself. The quiet of someone from whom something structural has been removed — the certainty he has been building his identity around, gone, and now he has to find out what's underneath. He doesn't know what's underneath. That is the actual terror.

What lands, in the silence, is not just the founding narrative. It is everything that came before the bunker. The external variables defense collapses here too. Naum and Kess weren't anomalies the machine couldn't predict — they are evidence that the machine cannot predict what a person needs in an open world, because it only holds the world it was trained on. The machine sent him out equipped for a journey it could model. The journey it could model was not this one. There is no version of the calculation that would have gotten him here correctly, because the machine cannot update. It cannot learn from what happened after. It has been answering the tribe's questions with the same frozen data for five generations, shaped only by the questions they knew to ask, unable to know what it doesn't know.

He was not failed by bad inputs. He was failed by a closed system trying to predict an open world.

He does not break dramatically. He depressurizes.

Tace is watching. She writes a single sentence in the journal and does not show it to anyone. Naum witnesses it and knows he will carry the weight of Marn's face in this moment for the rest of his life. He did not choose this. It happened to him. This is what the tradition asks.

---

## PART FOUR: THE RETURN

### Chapter 11 — The Road Back
*Different people descending the same mountain.*

They don't talk much on the way down. The silence has different textures for each of them.

Marn moves through it carefully, like someone relearning how to walk. He eats when Tace puts food in front of him. He checks Kess's feet without being asked and treats the worst of it without comment. The healer in him functions even when the rest of him doesn't. He is, practically speaking, still useful. He doesn't know what to do with this.

Naum carries two documents. One for the Recorder. One for himself — the weight of Marn's crisis, which he witnessed and which is now part of him, which is what the tradition actually meant when it asked him to bring back something true.

Kess walks barefoot as always. She already knows how this ends.

Tace leaves a cache note in a hollow tree on a known hunting route: *Going to be later than expected. Tell Callum the Not Deer are ranging north. I'm fine.*

---

### Chapter 12 — The Longhouse
*Naum returns. Naum becomes.*

The Recorder listens to the whole of it without expression, without interruption. Naum tells it the way it happened — not the glory story he left expecting, but the true one. The machine that dreamed it remembered everything. The girl who could feel the weight of truth. The young man who lost his home without moving an inch. And the second half of a story the archive has held incomplete for three generations.

The Recorder has never received a story like this before — an account that completes an existing record, that shows what an old event set in motion, that connects a beginning to its consequence. He sits with it.

Then he calls for the Scribe.

She comes already knowing. She has been waiting — for what, she couldn't have said. She bares the last unmarked skin on the Recorder's forearm and looks at Naum once.

*"They've traded wisdom for certainty."*

She nods. She begins.

Naum is a man. He doesn't feel what he expected. He feels something quieter and heavier and more real.

---

## CODA

Marn stands in the doorway of the longhouse. He watches the needle work. He has not decided whether he is staying. The decision is not about logistics. It is about whether the person built by the True Keeper's knowledge is someone he wants to remain.

Kess sits against the outside wall, her gourd banjo across her knees. She already knows what he decides. She is the only one who does.

Tace is in the yard, crouching over something in the dirt — a track, a mark, something that requires closer examination. Her pencil moves. The journal is open. She does not look up.

The archive has a new entry.

The hollow will leave her a cache.

---

*End.*

## STRUCTURAL NOTES

**Length:** The arc supports a tight novel (70–90k words) or a limited series of 6–8 episodes. The bunker sequence and the longhouse return both lend themselves to visual storytelling.

**POV question:** Whether this is Naum's POV exclusively or braided among the four is undecided. Braided allows Marn's crisis to land harder; single-POV keeps the coming-of-age arc more focused. A possible compromise: Naum primary, with close-third limited sections from Kess and Marn at key moments.

**The open question that shapes everything:** What is Tace looking at in the final image? What goes in the journal? This is the ending's last beat and it should be specific — something she has been trying to catalogue for years that Kess, without meaning to, has just explained.
