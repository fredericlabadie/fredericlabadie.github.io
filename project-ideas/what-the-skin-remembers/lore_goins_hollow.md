# GOINS HOLLOW
## What the Skin Remembers — Lore Document

---

### THE PLACE

Goins Hollow sits in a fold of the mountains that most travelers pass without noticing. The hollow runs roughly northeast to southwest, sheltered on both sides by ridges dense enough with hemlock and oak that the sky appears as a strip from the valley floor. A cold creek runs through it year-round. The land is difficult to farm and easy to defend and has been home to the same extended community for as long as anyone in the hollow can remember, which is longer than the Collapse.

The name is old. There is a standing joke in the community about all the coming and going — travelers come and go, seasons come and go, and Goins Hollow is still here. The name is both family name and description, which is the kind of coincidence that gets worn into a permanent piece of local identity.

The official pre-Collapse name of the area is not remembered by anyone currently living there, which is not considered a loss.

---

### THE PEOPLE

The community of Goins Hollow is Melungeon by heritage — a convergence of Scots-Irish, Portuguese, African, and Cherokee lineages that predates the Collapse by centuries. The Melungeon communities of the Appalachian region were isolated and self-sufficient long before isolation and self-sufficiency became survival requirements. The Collapse was not the first time the world had decided people like them didn't matter. They had practice.

Five generations out, the community is what extended isolation and careful endogamy produces: a tight, coherent culture with strong internal bonds, deep local knowledge, significant social capital, and genuine difficulty trusting outsiders. They are not hostile to travelers — they trade, they share information, they offer shelter to people in genuine need. But full belonging is earned slowly and possibly not at all in a single lifetime.

The community knows its own letters. This was a point of pride before the Collapse and remains one — not because literacy is essential to their practice the way it is for the Recorder or True Keeper tribes, but because it is connected in their cultural memory to a time when being able to read and not being believed was its own kind of wound. They read. They have always read.

---

### WHAT THEY KNOW

The knowledge base of Goins Hollow is practical, deep, and distributed. No single person carries all of it — it lives in the community collectively, which is by design.

**Plant medicine.** Five generations of Appalachian plant knowledge, maintained and extended by each generation. They know what grows where and when, what is medicine and what is poison, what treats infection and what induces it, what is for spiritual work and what is for physical work. The distinction between those last two is more porous in Goins Hollow than in communities with harder epistemological lines. A plant that calms the nervous system is also a plant that quiets the part of you that's too loud to hear the forest. These are not considered different applications.

**The Wyrd.** The community has been living adjacent to anomalous phenomena for five generations and has developed practical knowledge about it — not theological, not systematic, but accumulated. They know which parts of the hollow to avoid after dark and which seasons the Not Deer are more active. They know what draws them and what doesn't. This knowledge is oral, imprecise, and largely accurate because it has been tested against reality continuously for a hundred years. Tace grew up with this knowledge as one layer of what was known, and then began building another layer on top of it.

**Survival craft.** Rope-making, hide-tanning, cold-smoking, water management, construction in difficult terrain. The hollow demands specific skills and the community has them. Visitors sometimes mistake competence for simplicity. This is a mistake.

---

### THE COMMUNITY'S RELATIONSHIP TO TACE

Tace is one of theirs in the fullest sense — known since birth, trusted, held in genuine affection. She is also, in a mild way that nobody says directly, understood to be a specific kind of person who does a specific kind of thing that is not exactly like what anyone else does.

Other people in Goins Hollow know the forest. Tace studies it. The distinction is recognized without being fully understood. Her field journals — accumulated over years of walkabouts — are considered valuable to the community in the abstract and are practically consulted about twice a year when something specific comes up. The rest of the time they are Tace's thing.

When she goes out, people leave caches for her. This is not organized — different people do it independently, without coordination, when they happen to be in areas she frequents. A wrapped bundle wedged in the fork of a known tree. Dried meat and a small knife and occasionally a note. The notes are practical: *bad weather coming from the northwest, be careful on the ridge.* Sometimes just: *we saw you last week on the far side of the creek. You looked fine.* She has been finding these caches since she was a teenager and she still finds them now. She does not find this sentimental. She finds it efficient.

She is expected back eventually. Eventually is flexible.

---

### WHAT THE COMMUNITY DOESN'T KNOW

They do not know that Tace has gone further out than she has ever gone before on this particular walkabout, because she followed something she needed to understand and then followed it further, and she is now traveling with three strangers in the direction of a federal bunker in the high country.

They would find this unsurprising. They would probably leave an extra cache.

---

### GOINS HOLLOW AND THE OTHER TRIBES

Goins Hollow has trading relationships with several communities in the region and is considered a reliable source of plant medicine and wilderness knowledge. They are respected without being central to anyone's politics, which is a position they have cultivated carefully.

They are aware of the Recorder tribe — they have traded with them, and some community members have made the journey to the longhouse with stories they considered worth telling. They respect the tradition without being part of it.

They are aware of the True Keeper tribe and are skeptical of them in the comfortable way of people who have never fully trusted institutional authority and have been proven right often enough to feel settled in that skepticism. The idea of a machine that knows everything is not one Goins Hollow finds compelling. They have seen what happens when the thing holding all the knowledge breaks.

---

### A NOTE ON THE MELUNGEON HERITAGE

The Melungeon identity of Goins Hollow is present in the community's culture without being its defining story. Five generations of post-Collapse life has layered over everything — the community's primary identity is *the hollow*, not any pre-Collapse category. But the convergence that produced them is visible in faces, in naming patterns, in the texture of their knowledge base, in a specific quality of stubborn persistence that everyone in the hollow recognizes as characteristic without tracing it to a source.

Tace carries this without thinking about it, the way you carry the rhythm of your first language in every other language you learn.
