# KESS — Character Document
## Folk Practice, Tics & Behavioral Notes

---

### THE INHERITANCE

Kess's practice isn't a single tradition. It's three or four traditions that stopped being separate somewhere around her great-grandmother's generation and fused into something with its own internal logic. Scots-Irish second sight and fairy obligation. African American hoodoo and rootwork — the crossroads, the conjure bag, the language of the dead. Cherokee and Shawnee plant knowledge and the understanding that right relationship with the land is not metaphor, it's maintenance. The gourd banjo her mother's mother kept on the wall came from somewhere none of them could name anymore, but the tuning was older than Appalachia — older than the mountains, older than the people the mountains were named after, carrying the memory of the akonting and the ngoni in its open strings without anyone left alive who knew to say so.

She doesn't think of any of this as separate. It's just how the world is structured. The invisible world has weight and expectation, and you either maintain your obligations to it or you don't, and if you don't, things go wrong in ways you won't be able to explain.

Her gift — the truth-sense, the physical pressure of lies and true things in her chest — she understands as the Sight. Second sight, in the Scots-Irish sense, means you see what others can't. In the hoodoo sense it means you're close enough to the other side that information passes through. She doesn't separate these explanations. They're both describing the same thing from different angles.

### PHYSICAL TICS

**No shoes.** Ever, or as close to never as survival allows. She believes — and isn't wrong — that the ground tells you things. Her gift is embodied; it lives in her chest and her hands and the soles of her feet. Insulation is the enemy of information. She has calluses like boot leather. In winter she wraps her feet in cloth but won't put anything with a sole between her and the earth if she can help it. When she's forced to wear shoes, she's visibly uncomfortable, slightly off, like an instrument slightly out of tune.

**Never looks up into trees after dark.** This is absolute. If you ask her why, she'll say only: *you might see something you can't unsee.* She won't elaborate. She's not being dramatic. Something her grandmother told her and her grandmother believed it, and Kess has had no evidence to doubt it. She angles her eyes down or straight ahead after sunset in any wooded space.

**Won't whistle in the forest.** Won't whistle after dark anywhere. Whistling in the woods calls things. She doesn't specify what things. When Naum whistles a walking tune on the trail she'll go silent and slightly ahead of him, putting distance between herself and whatever he may have just called.

**Counts crows.** Involuntarily and under her breath. *One for sorrow, two for joy, three for a letter, four for a boy, five for silver, six for gold, seven for a secret never to be told.* She'll stop mid-sentence to count if crows cross her path. She takes the results seriously. A seven-crow morning means she moves carefully all day and says little.

**Tells the bees.** If the party encounters a hive — feral colony in a tree hollow, old managed hive gone wild — she'll stop and speak quietly to it. Major events must be reported. Deaths, especially. Alliances. Significant changes in the group. The tradition holds that bees are connected to the dead and if they aren't kept informed they leave, and things fall apart when the bees leave. She does this without ceremony, almost conversationally, the way you'd update a neighbor.

**Won't burn sassafras.** If someone reaches for a piece of sassafras wood for the fire she'll take it from their hand without explanation and set it aside. She won't say why unless pushed, and when pushed she'll just say *not that wood* in a tone that closes the subject.

**Bedroll orientation.** Every camp, every night, she arranges her bedroll so her feet point away from any entrance — door, gap, tent opening. The dead are carried out feet first. You don't sleep feet-to-door. She does this without comment and without fail. After a few weeks on the trail, Naum starts doing it too. He doesn't tell her.

**Stops at crossroads.** Any significant intersection — roads, trails, rivers meeting — she pauses. She turns in a slow circle, listening. She leaves something small: a pinch of tobacco, a coin, a single hair. The crossroads is a threshold between worlds in both the hoodoo tradition she carries and the Scots-Irish one. Eshu/Legba opens roads and closes them. The crossroads is where you leave debts and pick up knowledge. She never passes through one carelessly.

**Her shadow.** She won't let her shadow fall across a sick person or an open wound. This is instinctive, semi-conscious — she angles herself without thinking. If someone is seriously injured she stays to their north, adjusting with the sun.

### THE WHISKEY

She carries a clay bottle of whiskey she made herself or inherited — she doesn't say which. She'll share food, water, warmth, labor, anything asked of her. She will not share the whiskey and she will not explain it calmly if someone helps themselves. It's for the little people. The Scots-Irish tradition of leaving offerings for the fae merged in her line with the hoodoo tradition of libations for the dead and the loa — the spirits who must be fed if you want passage through their territory. She doesn't make a sharp distinction between fairies and loa and the honored dead. They're all part of the invisible world that she's in closer contact with than other people. You maintain right relations. You leave the whiskey.

When they reach a significant threshold — the bunker, a ruin of consequence, any place where something terrible happened — she'll pour a small measure at the entrance before going in. Not dramatically. The way you knock before entering.

### THE MOJO BAG

She wears a small cloth bag around her neck, inside her shirt. Knotted with red thread. She made it herself from instructions passed down through her mother's line, which was working hoodoo before the Collapse and after it with equal pragmatism. The bag contains: graveyard dirt from her mother's grave, a small bone, a root she won't name, something else she won't show anyone.

She will not let anyone touch it. Not even Naum. Not as a rule she explains — she simply moves away if a hand comes near it, the way you'd protect a wound. The working is sealed. Outside hands break it. If the bag were lost or destroyed she would spend a significant portion of whatever resources she had replacing it before she did anything else.

### GRAVEYARD WORK

When she passes a grave — marked or unmarked, she can usually tell — she takes a small pinch of dirt from it and leaves a coin or a pinch of tobacco. Graveyard dirt is one of the most powerful substances in rootwork; it carries the knowledge and the force of the person beneath it. You take it with respect and you compensate the dead for it. She collects different kinds for different purposes: dirt from a child's grave for innocence-work, from a soldier's grave for protection, from the grave of someone who died unjustly for justice-work. She keeps them in separate small pouches she doesn't mix.

She talks to the dead matter-of-factly. Not dramatically, not with ceremony, just as a form of address. When she passes a marked grave she might say the person's name if she can read it, note the date, say something brief and practical. *Long time. Thank you for the dirt.* This startles people who don't know her. She finds their startlement baffling. The dead are just people who aren't here anymore. You still have manners.

### BONE READING

She carries several small bones — bird bones mostly, one that might be from something larger — wrapped in cloth. When she's uncertain about a direction or a decision, she'll throw them and read the pattern. She doesn't do this publicly if she can avoid it and she doesn't explain the system, which is partially inherited and partially her own. It involves the direction the bones point, which ones touch, which land face up. The reading takes her about thirty seconds and she acts on it without discussion. She's been doing it long enough that she mostly trusts it. She's occasionally wrong, and when she is she re-examines the throw rather than the method.

---

### PLANT KNOWLEDGE

She knows the medicinal and spiritual uses of a significant number of Appalachian plants, which were in her line before the Collapse and are considerably more useful after it. Black cohosh, yellowroot, bloodroot, may apple, snakeroot. She knows which are medicine and which are poison and that several are both depending on dose and preparation. She knows which plants are associated with which kinds of spiritual work. She knows that some plants must be asked before you take them, and she asks.

She also knows which plants protect against what. A sprig of something tucked into a doorframe. A root buried at a threshold. She does these things at camp without announcement and other people's skepticism is not something she wastes energy on.

### THE GOURD BANJO

She carries it on her back, wrapped in oilcloth when it rains. The gourd is old — dried hard, dark with handling, cracked once along the shoulder and repaired with pine pitch that's held for longer than she's been alive. Four strings, gut, replaced when they break with whatever she can source. The neck is hardwood, the tuning pegs wooden and imprecise. It stays slightly out of tune and she corrects it by ear before she plays, which she can do in the dark.

The playing style she inherited is not bluegrass — it predates bluegrass by a long way. She plays it flat or cradled in her lap, the notes picked up from the melody string while the others drone underneath, a sound that's closer to the African instruments it descended from than anything you'd hear at a modern picking session. She doesn't know this. She just knows how her grandmother's hands moved and she learned to move hers the same way.

She plays in the evenings when the camp is settled and the obligations of the day are met. Not for an audience — she plays facing slightly away from the others, toward the treeline or the dark. The music is partly communication. She plays the songs she was taught and some she's made herself, and a few she only plays alone, quietly, that she calls *the old ones* — songs with words she doesn't know the meaning of, only the sound. Remnants of West African language that crossed the Atlantic in the mouths of enslaved people and survived in Appalachian folk tradition long after anyone knew what they were saying. She sings them phonetically, passed down generation to generation as pure sound. They feel important. She trusts that.

She stops playing completely when she's frightened — not silence, absence, which is different. The cessation of the banjo is the clearest signal she gives that something is wrong.

She has specific songs for specific situations: one she plays while working, rhythmic and low, that keeps her hands steady. One for the dead, slower, that she plays when she tells the bees or leaves graveyard dirt. One she picks under her breath in a place where something bad happened — her grandmother called it *the sweeping song*, because it's what you play to move something out. And one she has never named and plays only when she's alone, that she learned from no one and doesn't remember learning, that she has been playing since before she was old enough to know she knew it.

### HOW THE OTHERS READ HER

**Naum** finds her practices instinctively respectful before he finds them meaningful. He was raised in a tradition of stored memory and witnessed truth; Kess's relationship to the invisible world isn't foreign to him, just different in its grammar. He starts adapting small behaviors — the bedroll, pausing at crossroads — without discussing it with her. At some point on the journey he takes her seriously in a way he couldn't have articulated before they started.

**Marn** was raised by people who treat non-computational knowledge as noise. Her practices irritate him in specific proportion to how often they seem to be correct. He asks once, early in the journey, what the empirical basis is for the crow-counting. She looks at him for a long moment and says: *what's the empirical basis for asking a machine what's true?* He doesn't have a good answer. He doesn't bring it up again.

### THE GIFT IN HER OWN TERMS

She does not think of herself as having a mutation. The word would mean nothing to her. She thinks of herself as someone with the Sight — which in her tradition means you're close enough to the border of the visible and invisible worlds that information passes through you, whether you want it to or not. The hoodoo tradition she carries has a name for this too: she'd call it being *called to the crossroads*, meaning the crossroads is not just a place she passes through but a state she exists in.

This is not comfortable. She is permanently closer to the membrane than other people. She feels everything at a higher intensity. The whiskey for the little people, the graveyard coins, the mojo bag, the stopped at crossroads — all of it is maintenance. You tend your obligations so the invisible world doesn't pull you under. She's been managing this since she was small enough that her mother had to explain why she couldn't sit at the dinner table when her father was lying about the harvest. It has never been easy. It has also never been something she could put down.

What changes on this journey is that for the first time it isn't just a burden she manages. It's a tool the world needs her to use. That is new. She doesn't know yet what to do with it.

---

*Kess does not explain herself. She maintains her obligations. She is twelve years old and she is older than anyone in the room.*
