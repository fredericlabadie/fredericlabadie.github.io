# TACE
## Character Document — Folk Practice, Tics & Behavioral Notes

---

### THE NAME

Tace. From an old Quaker practice word meaning *be silent*. In Goins Hollow it is simply a name, the meaning worn off through generations of use. She does not know the etymology. She would find it accurate if told.

---

### WHO SHE IS

Tace is mid-to-late twenties, lithe in the way of someone who moves constantly and forgets to eat when absorbed in something. Her hair is dark and kept braided or bunned not from preference but because it gets in the way of her work otherwise. She has a particular quality of stillness when she is watching something — not tension, just attention, the complete attention of someone who has learned that looking carefully costs nothing and misses things.

Her skin tells the story of a life lived impractically. There are tan lines in the wrong places: forearms dark, shoulders pale, the underside of her wrists sometimes burned where she rolled her sleeves up without thinking and handled something she should have covered for. Small scars on her shins from moving through brush without considering her legs. A faint chemical mark on two fingers of her right hand from a plant that stains and doesn't wash off completely. Strange calluses in specific places that suggest repeated tasks done without the proper tools. She always planned to be back before this became a problem, and she was wrong at a rate she has stopped being surprised by.

Her clothing is always somewhat wrong for the situation. Many pockets, all full. The contents of the pockets are not entirely logical — specimen bags and field tools alongside survival gear that was thrown in as an afterthought. There is always cordage on her somewhere, usually hand-twisted, usually more of it than seems necessary. She has a firestarter that is extremely reliable and a second one for backup and has still managed to get caught without fire twice.

---

### THE FIELD JOURNAL

She keeps a field journal that is not like Aldous's. Aldous's journal is notation, index, record. Tace's journal is observational drawing, pressed specimens, sketched anatomy, written descriptions that are partly scientific and partly the attempt to think on the page. She goes through journals at a rate that is slightly alarming given how hard paper is to source. She keeps the old ones in Goins Hollow — there is a shelf of them, a decade of shelf, that the community regards with a mixture of pride and bewilderment.

Pens and pencils are her most critical resource and she treats them accordingly — which means as consumables, not as treasures. She burns through them at a rate that alarms people who don't know how much she writes. A pen is a tool. When it runs out it is gone and she needs another one. She carries multiple at various stages of depletion, trades for them when she can, and maintains a low-grade awareness of her current stock the way someone else might track food or water. Finding a pen that works well produces genuine, slightly embarrassing pleasure. Running out in the middle of something produces a specific controlled frustration she has learned to manage by switching to whatever marks — charcoal, clay, berry-stain on bark — until she can resupply.

She does not form attachments to specific pens. They run out. This is their nature. She has solved this problem the only way she knows how: carry more.

She does not fully understand that other people relate to objects differently until the incident with Aldous's pen — a family gift carried from ARDEN, refillable, the kind of thing ARDEN's institutional resources can produce and maintain and that she has never encountered. She picks it up because it is there and she needs to write something. She sets it down somewhere else. When she sees his face, she does the generous thing: she offers him three of hers. The offer makes it worse.

From his perspective, she has treated a precision instrument that doesn't run out like it's interchangeable with three things that will each be depleted in a week — which is, from his perspective, exactly the careless and wasteful relationship to resources he has been watching her demonstrate since she joined the party. He is wrong about the carelessness. He is not wrong about the waste, from where he's standing.

From her perspective, she has no category for a pen that refills. The solution to pens running out is more pens. She offered him more pens. She cannot parse why this is the wrong answer until much later, when she actually examines his pen closely and understands what she's looking at — at which point she goes quiet for a while and then asks, carefully, how you refill it. He tells her. She files this under a heading she keeps open for a long time: *things the hollow doesn't have that I didn't know to want.*

She also carries one book: a pre-Collapse text on the scientific method, water-stained and rebound twice, that she found in a cache at fourteen and has carried since. She has read it more times than she can count. The margins are full. She would not trade it for anything. She does not fully realize this makes her similar to Aldous in a way that would initially irritate both of them.

### HOW SHE KNOWS WHAT SHE KNOWS

The community of Goins Hollow gave her a foundation: plant names, seasonal patterns, which things in the forest to be careful around and why, practical animal knowledge. She received this the way all children in the hollow do — in passing, by being present, through the accumulated context of a life lived in that place.

Then she started adding to it herself, systematically, which is not something most people in Goins Hollow do.

Her method is observation, record, hypothesis, and return to test. She goes out, she watches, she writes down what she sees, she develops a tentative explanation, and then she goes back and looks again with the explanation in mind and finds out whether it holds. This is the scientific method as described in her battered book, applied to an ecosystem that the old world's scientists mostly studied before the Collapse and that has changed significantly since. She is producing original knowledge. She does not fully think of it this way. She thinks of it as trying to understand things.

The Wyrd is the frontier of her catalogue. Everything she encounters out there — the Not Deer, the crossroads phenomena, the things in trees after dark — goes into the journal in the same spirit as everything else: described precisely, compared to prior observations, held as open as possible until the pattern becomes clear. Some patterns are clear. Some have been open for years. Kess is the first thing she has encountered that broke her model entirely.

---

### HOW SHE JOINS THE GROUP

She has been observing the group from a cautious distance for two days before she decides to do anything about them.

What she observes: three young people traveling in a direction that is going to take them through territory she knows is currently dangerous, with a level of Wyrd awareness that ranges from surprisingly competent (the child) to negligible (the tall one with the bow). She makes a pragmatic decision. These people are going to get themselves killed, and she was going to go that direction anyway.

She steps out of the trees in front of them, introduces herself minimally, and begins traveling with them.

The moment that decides her is watching Kess. She sees Kess stop at a crossroads, turn slowly, leave something small. She has seen folk practices around crossroads before — Goins Hollow has them too — but the quality of Kess's attention is different. It is not a ritual performed by rote. It is a person in genuine exchange with something present. Tace has no category for this. She files it under *requires further observation* and does not stop looking.

She has been cataloguing the Wyrd as biology. Kess is, apparently, a human being who is part of it. This breaks Tace's taxonomy in a way she finds both deeply uncomfortable and completely compelling.

### PHYSICAL TICS & BEHAVIORS

**She crouches to look at things.** Constantly and without self-consciousness. Footprints, plants, scat, disturbed soil, strange marks on bark. She crouches, looks closely, and sometimes does not get up for a while. The rest of the party learns to wait.

**She touches things to test them.** Bark, mushrooms, berries — not to eat them, to feel texture and resilience, to look at what comes off on her fingers. She knows which tests are safe. She has also miscalculated this twice, which accounts for two of the stains.

**She narrates what she's observing under her breath.** Not loudly, not for audience. Thinking out loud while she's looking at something. If you ask her to repeat it she usually can't.

**She forgets to eat.** On ordinary days she eats when the group eats and doesn't think about it. When she's absorbed in something — following a track, examining a specimen, trying to understand something new — she stops registering hunger entirely. The first person to notice this and start putting food in front of her anyway is Aldous, which surprises them both.

**She takes things without registering that she's taking them.** This is not theft in her understanding and it is genuinely not malicious — it is a category error that goes all the way down. In Goins Hollow, things circulate. The relevant categories for an object are *available* and *needed*, not *mine* and *yours*. The cache system is this principle externalized: someone left it because they had it, she takes it because she needs it, the ledger is communal and invisible. Growing up inside this commons means she never fully developed the internal accounting that most people run automatically. She will pick up Aldous's pen to write something and not return it. She will take a portion of food from a pack without distinguishing between shared supplies and someone's personal stores. She will pocket an interesting tool from a ruin with the same uncomplicated ease she'd pick up a stone. She is not watching herself do it. When someone asks for something back, she hands it over without drama or apology, because she genuinely does not register that she did something worth apologizing for. Aldous, who keeps a detailed inventory, finds this profoundly disorienting. Noan witnesses it with the careful attention of someone who has learned that what seems like a character flaw is sometimes a window into a world built differently. Kess doesn't have enough possessions for it to come up.

**She is not afraid of the dark.** Specifically: she doesn't performatively not-fear it, she simply doesn't fear it. The dark is where certain things are more visible. She has spent enough time in it to know its rhythms. This reads as unnerving to people expecting a normal response to darkness and she has stopped noticing the effect.

**She does not look up into trees after dark.** This rule is Kess's, carried by Kess's tradition. But Tace independently does not look up into trees after dark, for reasons that are in the journal under a heading she has not yet closed. They have never discussed this. They do not need to.

### TACE AND THE COMMUNITY

Goins Hollow holds her in the loose, warm, slightly exasperated way of a family that has one member who cannot be contained. She is theirs fully. She is also persistently elsewhere.

The caches are the community's expression of this: we know where you go, we know you don't plan ahead, we have decided to take care of you anyway. She finds them and she is efficient about receiving them. She writes notes and leaves them in the cache locations for whoever will find them. *Saw the Not Deer again on the east side of the ridge. Three sightings. They seem to be ranging further north than last season. Tell Callum.* The notes are always practical. She considers this communication.

She is expected back eventually. She will be back eventually. These facts are held simultaneously without anxiety by everyone in the hollow who knows her, which is everyone.

---

### TACE AND KESS

Kess is the first human being Tace has encountered who is, by any reasonable accounting, part of the Wyrd. Not adjacent to it. Not studied in it. Of it.

This breaks something open in Tace that she is still working out. Her catalogue has a section for things she cannot yet explain. Kess gets a section. It is the longest section.

What develops between them is not mentorship in the usual sense — Kess is not a student and does not need instruction in how to be what she is. It is more like parallel observation: two people who see the same forest through different instruments, comparing notes. Tace watches Kess's methods work and files it under data. Kess watches Tace's methods work and files it under something she doesn't have a name for but recognizes as kin to her own.

Tace is a version of what Kess might become if the world is survivable: someone whose strangeness has become a vocation, who has found a way to make the thing that sets her apart into the thing she offers. Kess does not know this yet. Tace does not know she is modeling it.

---

### TACE AND MARN

The most intellectually productive collision in the party.

Tace uses empirical methods. Aldous uses empirical methods. Their data sets are different in ways that matter enormously. She knows things the machine doesn't. He has recorded things in the wild that contradict the machine's outputs without yet admitting that's what they are.

She is not hostile to the True Keeper as an institution — she is indifferent to it in the way she is indifferent to anything she doesn't have direct evidence about. When Aldous begins asking her questions, she answers them with the same completeness she brings to everything: here is what I observed, here is when, here is what I concluded, here is what I haven't concluded yet.

He is the first person who has been interested in her field journals in a serious way. She does not know what to do with this. She handles it by continuing to answer questions.

---

### WHAT A WRITER NEEDS TO KNOW ABOUT TACE

She does not explain herself. She explains what she is observing. These are not the same thing.

She is not performing toughness. She is not performing anything. She is just operating at her natural level of function, which happens to be higher than most people expect, and she has stopped managing others' expectations of it.

She joined this party because they needed her and she was going that way anyway. She will stay because Kess is the most interesting thing she has ever encountered. She will not say either of these things directly. She will, instead, continue to be present and useful and to put food in front of people who have forgotten to eat.

The hollow will leave her a cache when she gets back. She will find it and feel, briefly, the weight of being known. Then she will move on.
