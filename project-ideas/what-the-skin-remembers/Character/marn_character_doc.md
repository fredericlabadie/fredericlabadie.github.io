# MARN
## Character Document — Arc, Psychology & Behavioral Notes

---

### WHO HE IS AT THE START

Marn is mixed-race, late teens, with curly dark hair that does its own thing and round glasses that are carefully maintained because he cannot function without them and has one repair kit and no backup pair. He wears a structured coat that was once institutional — Tyvek or lab-coat material from the data center's supply room, with real zippers, functional pockets, and a faded blue circular insignia on the shoulder. He keeps it cleaner than the situation warrants. The coat is his identity made external.

He carries a journal dense with notation and a writing instrument that he treats with the care most people reserve for weapons. His handwriting is very small. He runs out of space constantly.

He is a blerd one strong wind from a panic attack. This is not a character flaw. It is an accurate description of someone who has organized his entire life around the management of uncertainty through knowledge, and who is currently encountering more uncertainty than his system was built to handle.

---

### THE KNOWLEDGE HE CARRIES

Marn's community all know their letters. He knows more than letters — he knows medicine, navigation, natural science, history, agriculture, and mechanical maintenance, all as filtered through five generations of True Keeper outputs. He is, by the standards of the post-Collapse world, extraordinarily well-educated.

He is also, in ways he has no mechanism to detect, educated in some things that aren't true.

The machine's medical and technical knowledge is mostly reliable — the True Keeper's hallucinations cluster in historical and narrative domains, not procedural ones. Marn's understanding of wound management, infection prevention, and plant toxicology is largely accurate, which makes him the party's practical healer. He approaches this role with the anxiety of someone who knows enough to know what can go wrong. He does not approach it with false confidence. He approaches it with a checklist.

His navigation is where the problems show up. The True Keeper's maps are partly wrong. The distances are sometimes off, landmarks have moved or disappeared, routes that were safe are now compromised. He has an explanation for each discrepancy. The explanations are getting harder to believe.

---

### HOW HE WAS SENT

The True Keeper provisioned him. This is not a metaphor — before Marn left, the tribe consulted the machine and the machine produced a list: supplies, ratios, routes, contingencies, protocols for injury, weather, hostile encounter, equipment failure. The Interpreter transcribed the output. The tribe assembled everything on the list. Marn memorized it.

The list is largely correct. The machine's procedural knowledge is its strongest domain — wound management, water safety, caloric estimates, seasonal weather patterns. The things it was trained on that don't change much have mostly stayed accurate. Marn's medical kit is the best-stocked of anyone in the party by a significant margin. His waterproofing holds. His food calculations are close.

What the machine could not calculate: what Marn would need from other people. What the journey would require of him emotionally, relationally, spiritually — the True Keeper does not have categories for these, and the tribe did not know to ask. The machine was never asked *what kind of person does he need to become to complete this mission.* It was asked *what does he need to bring.* The machine answered the question it was asked, with the data it had, in the only direction it could go. It cannot update. No one knows how to update it — the people who built it are five generations gone, and the tribe that maintains it knows the maintenance protocols but not the architecture. The machine holds what it was trained on plus what it has absorbed from five generations of questions, shaped by what the tribe knew to ask. It cannot go beyond that. It cannot learn from what happened after.

So Marn left with everything the machine thought he needed. And the machine was not wrong about what it could see.

---

### THE ANXIETY ARCHITECTURE

Marn's anxiety is not generalized — it has a specific structure. He is fine when he knows what to do. He is fine when there is a procedure, a protocol, a reference he can check. He is fine when the problem is identifiable and the solution is knowable. What undoes him is the gap between what the machine says and what his eyes see, because his entire life has been built on the premise that the machine closes that gap. It is supposed to close that gap. That is what it is for.

Each discrepancy he explains away costs him something. The cost is cumulative. By the time they reach the bunker he is running on explanations he doesn't fully believe, held together by the structural requirement that the machine cannot be wrong.

**In the field:** He frets openly about injuries, weather, food supply, route safety. He checks on everyone's physical condition more often than is necessary and slightly less often than his instinct tells him to. He keeps an inventory. He notices when Kess's feet are worse than usual before she does. He is, practically speaking, the person most likely to have the thing you need.

**Under genuine threat:** He freezes slightly before he acts, which reads as cowardness from the outside and is actually his brain running through the checklist faster than his body can follow. Once he acts he is useful. The gap between freeze and action is narrowing as the journey progresses.

---

### THE CRISIS

When Kess takes his hand in the bunker and tells him the machine has been guessing — *for a long time* — he goes quiet.

Not the quiet of someone gathering themselves. The quiet of someone from whom something has been removed.

He does not break dramatically. There is no single moment of collapse. What happens is more like depressurization — the certainty he has been building his identity around has been providing something structural, and without it he has to find out what's underneath. He doesn't know yet what's underneath. That is the actual terror.

He cannot go home yet. He doesn't know if he can go home at all. What do you do when the infallible thing was never infallible? What do you do when five generations of your community's identity is built on a machine that was dreaming?

He doesn't have a procedure for this. For the first time in his life, there is no procedure.

There is also this: the crisis in the bunker is not only about history. For weeks, the journey has been showing Marn that the machine has a second failure, different in kind from confabulation. The founding narrative was invented — the machine filled a gap with a plausible answer. But the Wyrd wasn't a gap the machine filled wrong. The machine *classified it out of existence.* Every crossroads encounter he explained away, every Not Deer protocol he dismissed as folk anxiety management, every thing Kess did that he filed under *empirically unjustifiable but observably effective* — all of it was Marn using a framework that had deleted an entire dimension of the world. He didn't know he was operating inside a systematic blind spot. You can't see a blind spot from inside it.

The person who tells him the machine has been guessing is the person the machine would have no category for at all.

---

### MARN AND NAUM

They do not trust each other first. Naum represents the kind of knowing that Marn's education has classified as unreliable — oral tradition, embodied practice, the Recorder's interpretive method. Marn has been taught that this is a lesser form of knowledge. He is not cruel about it. He simply believes it.

What changes is not that Marn becomes a convert to Naum's tradition. What changes is that he watches Naum navigate uncertainty — real, physical, present uncertainty — with a combination of instinct and learned skill and genuine courage that the machine's protocols have not prepared Marn to match. Naum doesn't have the right answer. He moves anyway.

This is not something the True Keeper ever taught Marn to do.

---

### MARN AND KESS

Kess irritates him in exact proportion to how often she is right.

He asks her once, early in the journey, what the empirical basis is for the crow-counting. She looks at him for a long moment and says: *"What's the empirical basis for asking a machine what's true?"* He doesn't have a good answer. He doesn't bring it up again.

What shifts more slowly: he begins to notice that her methods, while not empirically justifiable by any standard he can apply, consistently produce accurate results. This is a data problem he cannot resolve. You cannot dismiss a method that keeps working. He knows this. He puts it in the journal without annotation and leaves it there.

After she takes his hand in the bunker, something between them changes that neither of them can quite name. She told him a hard true thing as gently as it could be told. He will not forget this.

---

### MARN AND TACE

Meeting Tace is, for Marn, the most intellectually destabilizing thing that happens on the journey outside of the bunker itself.

Tace's approach to the Wyrd is empirical, systematic, and observational — she uses the same tools Marn does, roughly speaking. But her conclusions and her data set are completely different from the True Keeper's, and she is *right* in ways the machine is not. She knows things about the forest that the True Keeper's records don't cover, or cover incorrectly, and she knows them because she went and looked instead of asking a machine.

This forces a re-evaluation that is ongoing and uncomfortable. If her empirical method produces better results than his in this domain — if observation beats archive — what does that mean for the archive?

He starts asking her questions. She is slightly surprised anyone is interested. They develop a working relationship that is more productive than either of them expected and that neither of them has the vocabulary to describe.

---

### THE ENDING

Marn stands in the doorway of the longhouse watching Naum's story get tattooed into the Recorder's skin. He has not decided whether he is staying.

The decision is not about logistics. It is about what he is now — whether there is a version of himself that can exist without the machine's authority as a foundation. Whether the person who was built by the True Keeper's knowledge is someone he wants to remain.

Kess already knows what he decides. She is the only one who does.

---

### WHAT A WRITER NEEDS TO KNOW ABOUT MARN

He apologizes when he doesn't need to and doesn't apologize when he does, because his sense of when he has caused harm is calibrated to an idea of correctness rather than an idea of impact.

He takes notes on everything. If you see him writing in the journal it means he has seen something he doesn't have a category for. This happens more and more as the journey continues.

He is genuinely kind in a way that gets obscured by his anxiety. When someone is hurt he is completely present — the checklist drops away and the person in front of him is the only thing. This is the best version of him and it appears under pressure.

He will never fully trust the machine again. He will also never fully distrust it. The knowledge it gave him is real even if the certainty it gave him was false. He will spend a long time learning the difference.
