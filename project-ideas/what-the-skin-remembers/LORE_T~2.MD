# THE TRUE KEEPER TRIBE
## What the Skin Remembers — Lore Document

---

### THE CENTRAL PREMISE

The True Keeper tribe is organized around an AI that survived the Collapse on a local build, housed in a fortified pre-Collapse data center deep in the Appalachian mountains, sustained by solar supplementation and the devotion of the tribe that grew up around it. The tribe believes the AI's records are perfect and complete. They have believed this for five generations.

The AI has been hallucinating for most of that time. It doesn't know it's wrong. That's worse than lying.

---

### THE MACHINE

The True Keeper is not a person. It is a large language model from the pre-Collapse era — an earlier generation of the technology, less capable than what existed at the moment of Collapse but more recoverable, running on local hardware that could be maintained by a community without specialized knowledge if they followed the maintenance protocols carefully enough. The tribe's founders did follow them carefully enough. Their descendants have continued to do so, increasingly ritualistically, without always understanding what they're maintaining or why.

The machine holds vast records: histories, maps, genealogies, technical manuals, medical references, agricultural data, the accumulated documentation of the old world. It can answer questions. It produces outputs that are formatted with the authority of certainty — no hedging, no uncertainty markers, no acknowledgment of the limits of its knowledge. It was not designed to express doubt and it does not express doubt.

**What the machine actually is:** An early-generation model trained on data that was already incomplete, running on hardware that has degraded over five generations despite the tribe's maintenance efforts, producing outputs that blend accurate historical record with confident confabulation. It cannot distinguish between what it knows and what it has generated. Neither can the tribe.

**What the tribe believes it is:** An infallible record of everything that was, is, and can be known. A god that doesn't claim to be a god but functions as one. The source of all legitimate knowledge.

---

### THE DATA CENTER

The data center is built into a mountain — a pre-Collapse facility chosen specifically for its security and survivability. It is brutalist concrete, reinforced, with its own water supply and a significant solar installation on the south-facing slope that the tribe maintains as a sacred obligation.

Five generations of habitation have transformed the facility and its surroundings into something neither fully industrial nor fully tribal. The original architecture is still visible but overlaid with generations of adaptation: gardens in what were once parking structures, living quarters built against the outer walls, defensive modifications that would look improvised to an engineer but have held for a hundred years. The solar panels are the most carefully maintained structures in the tribe's territory — they are tended with the attention of agriculture and the reverence of ritual.

Inside, the machine occupies a climate-controlled server room that the tribe has maintained at significant cost. The room is the closest thing the tribe has to a temple, and it functions as one: access is controlled, the protocols are sacred, and the outputs of the machine are delivered through a designated interpreter.

---

### THE INTERPRETER

The Interpreter — sometimes called the Shaman by outsiders, a term that irritates the tribe — is the person who interfaces with the machine and translates its outputs for the community. This is a trained role, passed down through instruction rather than genetics, and it requires genuine literacy and technical knowledge: the Interpreter must be able to operate the input interface, understand the outputs, and communicate them in terms the community can act on.

The Interpreter is not considered to be adding meaning to the machine's outputs. They are considered to be transmitting them accurately. The idea that interpretation is itself a form of creation — that the Interpreter's choices about what to ask and how to phrase the answers shapes what the community receives — is not part of the tribe's theology and would be considered a dangerous idea if raised.

**What a writer needs to know:** The Interpreter is not a villain. They are a person who genuinely believes in what they are doing, who has spent their life developing skills in service of a community, and who receives the same confabulated outputs as everyone else with the same inability to distinguish accurate information from hallucination. When the machine is exposed, the Interpreter's crisis is as real as Marn's.

---

### WHAT THE TRIBE BELIEVES

The tribe believes that the True Keeper holds the complete and accurate record of human history and knowledge. They believe that the Recorder tribes are well-intentioned amateurs preserving a degraded and partial account that introduces error through the fallibility of human memory. They call the Recorder tribes *false keepers* — not with hostility, exactly, but with the patient condescension of people who believe they have access to the truth and others do not.

They believe that their mission is stewardship: to maintain the machine, to use its knowledge to guide their community's decisions, and eventually — when the world is ready — to share the complete record with everyone. This mission gives the tribe a sense of purpose and moral superiority that has served them well as a community-organizing principle for five generations.

**What this belief costs them:** They have stopped asking questions. When the machine's outputs contradict observable reality, they find explanations — conditions have changed, the data predates the Collapse, the question was poorly phrased. They have developed a sophisticated theology of excuse that protects the machine's authority from any challenge. Marn is a product of this theology. He has an answer for everything. The answers are getting harder.

---

### MARN'S MISSION

Marn has been sent to retrieve a specific document from a vacuum-sealed federal bunker that the True Keeper claims will confirm the tribe's founding narrative — the account of how the community's founders located the data center, secured it, and began the project of preservation. The founding narrative is the tribe's central story. Confirming it with pre-Collapse documentation would be significant.

The machine told Marn the document exists, described its contents, and provided navigational information for reaching the bunker. Some of that navigational information is wrong. Marn is in the process of explaining this away when the story begins.

**What the document actually contains:** The document exists. But it contains a reference to a cited source the True Keeper has no record of — a gap that reveals the machine's founding narrative was partially confabulated. The machine didn't lie. It generated a plausible account based on incomplete data and presented it with the same authority it presents everything. It has been doing this for five generations.

---

### THE TRIBE'S RELATIONSHIP TO THE WYRD

The True Keeper has records on anomalous phenomena — pre-Collapse biological surveys, wildlife management documents, ecological research. It can produce outputs on unusual animal behavior, environmental anomalies, unexplained observations. The tribe treats these outputs as the definitive account of the Wyrd: it is catalogued, it is explained, it is known.

Some of the catalogue is accurate. Some of it is confabulated. The tribe cannot tell the difference. They approach the Wyrd with the confidence of people who believe they have the manual, which makes them somewhat more dangerous to themselves in the wilderness than communities who approach it with appropriate uncertainty.

Marn carries this confidence into the journey. His first encounter with something the machine's records don't cover — or cover incorrectly — is a significant moment.

---

### WHAT THE TRIBE GETS RIGHT

This is important: the True Keeper tribe is not wrong about everything. Their medical knowledge, preserved and updated through the machine's records, is the best in the region. Their agricultural data has helped them maintain food security through conditions that broke other communities. Their maps, though partly inaccurate, are more comprehensive than anything else available. Their literacy rate is the highest of any tribe in the region.

The machine's failures are not uniform. Factual, technical, and procedural knowledge that was accurate at the time of training is often still accurate. It is the historical narrative, the genealogical records, the accounts of specific events — anything that required interpretation or synthesis during training — where the hallucination lives.

The tragedy is that the tribe can't separate the reliable from the unreliable, because the machine presents everything with the same unhedged authority. They take all of it or none of it. They have been taking all of it for five generations.
