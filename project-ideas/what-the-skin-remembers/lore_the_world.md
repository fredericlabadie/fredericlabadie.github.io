# THE WORLD
## What the Skin Remembers — Lore Document

---

### FIVE GENERATIONS OUT

The Collapse happened roughly a hundred and twenty-five years ago. Nobody alive remembers it directly. Their grandparents' grandparents lived through it, and some of them left records — scratched into walls, stitched into cloth, tattooed into skin — but the records are incomplete and the people who could explain them are gone.

This is important: the world of the story is not a world in shock. It is not the immediate aftermath. The grief of the Collapse is the grief of a great-grandparent you never met — present in the shape of things, in absences and habits and warnings passed down until nobody remembers why, but not raw. Not fresh. The people in this story were born into this world. It is the only world they know, and mostly it seems normal to them.

What doesn't seem normal is the ruins.

---

### THE ANABASIS FEELING

The ruins are everywhere and they are incomprehensible.

Highways wide enough to land aircraft, overgrown with forest, their surfaces cracked and heaved by root systems that have had five generations to work. The bridges still standing are the ones built to outlast civilization, and they did — massive concrete spans over rivers, carrying nothing now but weather and the occasional crow. Cities have become quarries: the tribes that live near them harvest steel and glass and copper wire the way earlier peoples harvested flint. The downtown cores have become hills of rubble with trees growing through them.

The scale is the problem. A tribesman standing at the edge of what was once a stadium — its bowl now full of soil and scrub, its upper tiers still visible as concentric ridges in the hillside — has no framework for understanding what it was or what it held. The documents that survive are legible (the tribes that preserve literacy can read them) and almost entirely uninterpretable. A maintenance manual for an HVAC system. A university course catalogue. A child's birthday party invitation with a cartoon on it. The words are readable. The world they describe is gone.

This is not experienced as tragedy by the people in the story. It is wonder and bafflement in roughly equal measure. The ruins are just there, the way mountains are just there — impressive, slightly dangerous, occasionally useful, not mourned.

### WHAT SURVIVED

**Geography.** The mountains are still the mountains. The rivers still run the same courses, mostly. The forests have reclaimed a great deal of what was cleared, and the Appalachian ecosystem — which was old and resilient before the Collapse — has largely recovered. The land is healthy. That matters.

**Plant knowledge.** Herbalism, foraging, and agricultural knowledge survived in communities that never fully industrialized their food systems. The Appalachian tradition of plant medicine was strong enough before the Collapse that it carried forward, though it fractured and diverged as communities separated. Different groups know different things. What Kess knows and what Tace knows overlap but are not identical.

**Oral tradition.** The tribes that prioritized memory did better than the ones that didn't. Stories, songs, procedural knowledge, seasonal calendars — these survived in the mouths of people who understood that forgetting was a form of dying.

**Some technology.** Solar panels, where communities had the knowledge to maintain them. Water treatment infrastructure, in a few places, which has made those places powerful. Simple metalworking. The tribes that live near pre-Collapse manufacturing sites can sometimes produce things others can't.

**Literacy.** Unevenly. The Recorder tribe maintains it because their practice requires it — the Scribe must be able to read and write to perform her function. The True Keeper tribe requires it because the AI's outputs must be interpreted. Communities like Goins Hollow maintained it through the same stubborn self-sufficiency that got them through everything else. But there are tribes in the story's world that have lost it entirely, and they are not considered lesser for it.

---

### WHAT DIDN'T SURVIVE

**Scale.** Nothing operates at the scale of the old world. The largest communities in the Appalachian region number in the hundreds, not thousands. The logistics of feeding and organizing large populations collapsed along with everything else, and haven't recovered because there's been no pressure to recover them — the land can support what's here.

**Institutional medicine.** Germ theory survived in fragments — communities that kept the knowledge know to boil water, to clean wounds, to isolate the sick. But surgery, pharmaceuticals, anything requiring supply chains or specialized equipment is gone. People die of things that were trivially treatable a hundred and thirty years ago. This has shaped every culture in the story's world in ways they don't fully articulate because they don't know what they're missing.

**A shared history.** Five generations of separation has produced five generations of divergent memory. The Recorder tribe's history and the True Keeper tribe's history overlap, contradict each other, and contain gaps neither is aware of. There is no neutral record. There is no outside arbiter. The question of what actually happened — and what it means — is genuinely open, which is the condition the story is built on.

### TRIBAL ORGANIZATION

The Appalachian region is organized into loosely affiliated tribes ranging from small extended family groups to communities of several hundred. They trade, intermarry, conflict, and occasionally form alliances. There is no central authority and no mechanism for creating one — the attempt has been made and failed often enough that most tribes have stopped trying.

Territory is understood in terms of range rather than boundary. A tribe doesn't own the land up to a line; they know the land within a certain radius of their home ground, and that knowledge is itself a form of claim. The edges of territories are porous. Travelers are generally tolerated. Taking resources without asking is not.

The crossroads between territories are understood as neutral ground in most traditions — a carry-forward from both the practical reality (you need to be able to trade) and the spiritual one (the crossroads belongs to something older than any tribe).

---

### THE WYRD

The collapse of industrial civilization removed a great deal of noise from the ecosystem. What moved back in — or what became visible again without the noise — is a matter of genuine dispute between traditions.

The Wyrd is the word some communities use for the anomalous, the wrong, the things that live at the edges of the observable world. Not Deer. Things in trees after dark. Sounds that don't match any known animal. Crossroads phenomena. Whether these are supernatural entities, ecological mutations, pre-existing phenomena that were always there but ignored, or something the Collapse itself produced — nobody agrees, and in this world the disagreement is not academic. People make practical decisions based on their answer.

Kess's tradition treats the Wyrd as the invisible world made occasionally visible — spirits, obligations, entities that have always existed and must be maintained in right relationship.

Tace's tradition treats the Wyrd as a biological and ecological phenomenon that is not yet fully catalogued. She does not disbelieve in it. She takes notes on it.

The True Keeper has records on anomalous phenomena but they are incomplete and some of them are hallucinated.

Naum's tribe treats the Wyrd as something that happens to other people, until it doesn't.

---

### A NOTE ON TIME

Five generations is long enough that the Collapse functions as mythology rather than history for most people. The Recorder tribe is unusual in maintaining a systematic record that reaches back to firsthand accounts — their archive contains tattooed testimony from people who lived through the Collapse itself. This makes them both valuable and uncomfortable to other tribes. They are the closest thing the region has to an objective record.

They know this. It is the weight the Recorder carries on his skin.
