# WHAT THE SKIN REMEMBERS
### An Expanded Synopsis
*Working title. Character names are placeholders.*

---

Two generations after the Collapse, the Appalachian hills have swallowed the old world whole. The interstates are forest. The cities are quarries. What remains of human civilization has organized itself into something older — tribes, territories, oral law — and the question that matters most is not *how do we rebuild* but *how do we remember*.

---

## ACT ONE: THE SENDING

**CADE** is nearly eighteen, the age when young men of the Recorder's tribe go out into the world and come back as something other than boys. The tradition is specific: he must return with a *true story* — not a boast, not a rumor, but something witnessed and lived, something worth tattooing. The **Recorder**, an old man with no blank skin remaining, is the living archive of the tribe. His predecessors' preserved skins hang in the longhouse like pages in a book. When he dies, the Scribe — his wife — will add him to the collection. Every child of the tribe grows up knowing that their people's entire memory is held in human skin, which means memory is mortal, imperfect, and irreplaceable. It also means it's chosen. The Recorder doesn't record everything. He records what matters. What can be made to mean something.

The Recorder sends Cade out with no map and no instructions beyond the tradition itself: *go, witness, return*.

A few days into his journey, Cade comes down off a ridge into a holler and finds **WREN** — a girl of twelve or thirteen, sharp-eyed and watchful, living alone on a farm that's slowly failing. Her parents are recently dead. Her nearest kin is two days' walk and unlikely to want her. She knows the land around her farm and almost nothing else. Cade doesn't intend to bring her with him. But Wren has something that unnerves him: she seems to *know* when he's telling the truth.

She doesn't understand it herself. Her family's bloodline traces back to the early years after the Collapse, when a healer passed through the region with a CRISPR repair virus, injecting the most genetically degraded survivors — the product of desperate inbreeding — with engineered corrections. In Wren's line, several generations of isolation have twisted that intervention into something stranger. She can feel truth and falsehood the way you feel weather changing — as a pressure, a weight in her chest. Lies make her physically ill. Real things settle her.

She is not a comfortable person to be around. She is also, Cade realizes, a child with nowhere to go. He offers to bring her along — she can see more of the world than this one failed farm, and when he finds his story and turns back, she can come with him. She accepts before he finishes the sentence.

---

## ACT TWO: THE JOURNEY

They travel west and north, into the high country, through the ruins of the old infrastructure — a highway overpass colonized by a trading post, a valley controlled by a tribe that guards a working water treatment facility like a god. Cade is looking for something, though he couldn't say what. A story presents itself or it doesn't. The Recorder never said what kind of story; he only said it had to be true.

Ten days out, they encounter **DOLAN** — a young man of Cade's age, alone on the same rough trail. He is from the tribe of the **True Keeper**.

The True Keeper is not a person. It is a machine — an AI that survived the Collapse running on a local build, housed in a fortified pre-fall data center deep in the mountains, sustained by solar supplementation and the devotion of the tribe that grew up around it. It holds vast records: histories, maps, genealogies, technical manuals, the accumulated data of the old world. The tribe believes these records are perfect and complete. They call the Recorder tribes *false keepers* — mere flesh attempting what only code can do correctly. Their shaman, the machine's interpreter, speaks its outputs as prophecy.

Dolan has been sent on a mission. The True Keeper has told him there is a pre-Collapse archive in the mountains to the east — a repository of technical knowledge, infrastructure plans, the practical tools of civilization rebuilt. Dolan is to find it, inventory it, and return. He is certain of his mission the way only someone can be who has never been given reason for doubt.

He and Cade don't trust each other. But their paths overlap and the mountain is easier in company. They travel together with the careful wariness of people who want the same thing in the same direction for different reasons. Wren watches Dolan. Her gift is quiet around him — not because he's honest, exactly, but because he isn't lying. He believes everything he says. That, she will come to understand, is a different problem.

Small fractures appear as they travel. The True Keeper's guidance — relayed through Dolan's memorized instructions — doesn't match the terrain. Distances are wrong. A bridge the machine swore was there has been gone for a decade. Dolan explains each discrepancy away: the data is from before the Collapse, conditions have changed, the Keeper works in approximations that must be refined in the field. He has an answer for everything. The answers are getting harder.

Wren starts to feel it at night, lying still. A low-grade wrongness she can't locate. Not lies. Something worse — errors that don't know they're errors.

---

## ACT THREE: THE ARCHIVE

They find it: a collapsed university library, one wing still intact behind a fallen facade. Inside, by lantern light, they discover what the True Keeper told Dolan was there.

Except it isn't.

There are no infrastructure blueprints. No agricultural plans. No technical schematics for rebuilding civilization. What's there is something else entirely: student newspapers. Personal journals. A professor's handwritten notes, kept by candlelight, that stop mid-sentence when the power went out and never resumed. Photographs. A child's drawings someone kept. The accumulated record not of *what humanity knew* but of *what it felt like to be human at the end*.

The True Keeper told Dolan it had confirmed records of this archive's contents. Either it extrapolated wildly — or it invented.

Dolan doesn't accept this. He goes through the archive methodically, searching for what the machine said would be there, his movements becoming more agitated and less precise. Cade watches him and understands something: the Recorder's way — imperfect, embodied, interpretive — was never a lesser version of the machine's archive. It was a different thing entirely. The Recorder says *this is what we believe happened, and here is why it mattered*. The machine says *this is what happened*. But if the machine's facts are wrong, it has nothing. No meaning to fall back on. No "we believe." Just: wrong.

Wren puts her hand on Dolan's arm. She's been sick since they entered the archive, a low wave of nausea she's been swallowing back. She looks at him with the particular compassion of someone delivering news she knows will hurt.

*"It's been guessing,"* she says. *"For a long time."*

Dolan goes still. That kind of still that isn't calm — that's the absence of whatever was filling the space before.

He doesn't break. He goes quiet, which is its own kind of breaking. He can't go home yet. He doesn't know if he can go home at all. What do you do when the infallible thing was never infallible? When the certainty you built your self on was always just a machine dreaming?

Cade has his story. Not the one he expected — no monster, no treasure, no glory in the traditional sense. A story about a machine that dreamed it remembered everything. A girl who could feel the weight of truth like a hand pressed against her chest. A young man who lost his home without moving an inch.

He turns back toward the Appalachians.

---

## RESOLUTION

He doesn't know, walking back, whether the Recorder will find this story worth tattooing. It isn't glorious. Nobody won anything. But it's *true* — he witnessed it, lived it, and can account for it. That's what the tradition asks.

When he returns and tells it, the Recorder listens without speaking. When Cade is done, the old man is quiet for a long moment. Then he calls for the Scribe.

He bares the last remaining patch of unmarked skin on his left forearm.

*"They've traded wisdom for certainty,"* he says — the old insult, the old argument between tribes, now something closer to a eulogy — and he nods for her to begin.

Cade is a man.

Dolan stands in the doorway of the longhouse, watching the needle move. He hasn't decided yet whether he's staying.

Wren already knows.

## THEMES

**Memory as meaning-making vs. memory as data.** The central question of the story is not *what happened* but *how we hold it*. The Recorder's method — lossy, embodied, interpretive — is presented not as primitive but as honest about what memory actually is. The machine's method mistakes completeness for truth.

**"They've traded wisdom for certainty."** This line — the Recorder's critique of the True Keeper's tribe — is the thematic spine. Certainty without wisdom is fragile. Wisdom without certainty can survive error.

**Coming of age in a world of broken systems.** All three young people in this story inherit systems they didn't build: a tradition, a machine, a body that was edited before they were born. Growing up means deciding which of those inheritances to carry forward, and which to question.

**The body as archive.** The Recorder's method is extreme but coherent: they make memory mortal so that it remains meaningful. The act of tattooing is also an act of selection — choosing what's worth carrying in skin.
