# THE RECORDER TRIBE
## What the Skin Remembers — Lore Document

---

### THE CENTRAL PREMISE

The Recorder tribe preserves memory by tattooing their history onto the bodies of a lineage of living historians. When a Recorder dies, their preserved skin is added to the longhouse archive. Memory is mortal, imperfect, chosen, and irreplaceable.

This is not considered a compromise. It is considered the truth of what memory actually is.

---

### THE ARCHIVE OF SKIN

The longhouse at the center of the tribe's home ground contains the archive: the preserved skins of every Recorder going back as far as the tradition holds. They are mounted, treated, and kept in careful order — not displayed as trophies but stored as documents, with the reverence you would give to something that cannot be replaced.

The archive reaches back five generations to firsthand accounts of the Collapse. The oldest skins are the most fragile and the most sacred. They contain testimony from people who saw the old world end. Some of what they recorded is interpretable. Some of it refers to things that no longer exist and cannot be explained. The tribe has stopped trying to explain those passages and instead preserves them as-is — evidence of something that once mattered even if the context is gone.

Children of the tribe grow up knowing the archive is there. They are not taken to see it casually. Access is earned and purposeful. The weight of it — the knowledge that their entire history is held in human skin, which rots and burns and tears — is something every member of the tribe carries.

This is understood as appropriate. Memory should have weight.

---

### THE RECORDER

There is one Recorder at a time.

When someone is selected as Recorder, they give up their name. They were Tomis Franklin. They are now the Recorder. The name goes into the archive with everything else they were before — a private tattoo placed at the beginning of their tenure, their old name recorded once and never spoken again. They become a vessel. The story of the tribe flows through them, not from them.

The current Recorder has no blank skin remaining. His entire body is covered in the tribe's history — arms, legs, torso, neck, the backs of his hands, the tops of his feet. The progression of the tattooing tells its own story: the older work is faded and slightly blurred at the edges, the newer work sharp and dark. He is a palimpsest of everything that has happened in his lifetime and everything that came before.

He is old. He moves carefully. He does not speak casually about what is on his skin because not all of it is for casual conversation.

**What a writer needs to know about the Recorder:**
- He listens without expression and without interruption. He has done this for decades and the stillness is genuine, not performed.
- He does not validate stories as they are told. He waits. If a story is worth tattooing, it will still be worth tattooing after he has had time to consider it.
- He knows that his archive is incomplete. He has always known this. Events are recorded without their consequences, because tribesmen return with what they witnessed and no tribesman can witness everything. This is the central limitation of his method and he has made peace with it, until Naum returns with the second half of an old story and forces him to reckon with it fresh.
- He considers the truth of his own tribe when exposed to outside accounts. This is not comfortable.

---

### THE SCRIBE

The Scribe performs the tattooing. She is the Recorder's hands.

The current Scribe was Filamina Franklin. She took the role when her husband Tomis was selected as Recorder — she learned to read and write specifically for this purpose, at a point in their lives when neither of them could have known he would be chosen. She had the instinct before the occasion. When the occasion came, she was ready.

She gave up her name alongside his. They are the Recorder and the Scribe, which is not a diminishment — it is what they chose to become together, and they chose it together.

**What a writer needs to know about the Scribe:**
- Her hands are extraordinary. Decades of fine tattooing work have made them precise beyond what seems possible. She does not tremble. She does not rush.
- She is the one who decides the physical composition of each tattoo — where it goes on the skin, how large, what surrounds it. The Recorder selects what is worth preserving. The Scribe determines how it lives in the archive. This is a creative act and she understands it as such.
- She loved Tomis Franklin before he was the Recorder. She loves the Recorder now. These are not the same thing and she holds both without confusion.
- When Naum returns with his story, it is the Scribe who bares the last remaining patch of unmarked skin on the Recorder's forearm. She has been waiting for this. She does not say so.

### THE COMING-OF-AGE TRADITION

When a young person of the tribe comes of age, they go out into the world with a single instruction: return with a true story.

Not a boast. Not a rumor. Not something witnessed by someone else and passed along. Something seen with their own eyes, lived in their own body, that can be made to mean something. The Recorder determines whether a returned story meets the standard. He has refused stories before — not cruelly, but clearly. The tradition requires honesty about what counts.

The young person goes out with no map, no assigned direction, no timeframe. They come back when they have something worth bringing. Some are gone for weeks. Some for years. A few do not come back, and this grief is held by the tribe without revision — they died finding a story, which is not considered a waste, though it is considered a loss.

When a story is accepted, the tattooing is performed in front of the community. The young person is a man or a woman from that point forward. Their story lives in the archive.

**What this means for Naum:** He leaves expecting a story of glory. He does not know yet what kind of story finds him.

---

### WHAT GETS RECORDED AND WHAT DOESN'T

The Recorder records events, not opinions. Witnessed actions, not interpretations. The archive is not a philosophy — it is a testimony.

This is its strength: the stories are true. Every tattooed account is the firsthand testimony of someone who was there.

This is its fatal limitation: events are recorded without their consequences. A conflict is recorded by the tribesman who witnessed the beginning of it. What that conflict set in motion — the alliances it broke, the children it displaced, the grudges it planted that bloomed a generation later — those require someone who was there for the second half. And most tribesmen come home before the second half.

The Recorder knows his archive contains many beginnings with no middles and no ends. He has made peace with this as the nature of the form. What Naum brings back — the second half of a story already in the archive — is something the tradition has never produced before.

---

### THE LONGHOUSE

The longhouse is built to last. The community has been maintaining and extending it for five generations — there are structural elements that are original construction alongside repairs and additions that are clearly newer. The archive room is the oldest part. It smells of the tanning compounds used to preserve the skins and something else underneath — something older, organic, that nobody has been able to fully eliminate and has stopped trying to.

The community gathers in the longhouse for decisions, for rituals, for the return of tribespeople from their quests. When a story is accepted, the whole community is there.

The Recorder and Scribe live adjacent to the longhouse, not in it. Their home is plain and well-made. There are no decorations. Everything in it has a purpose.
