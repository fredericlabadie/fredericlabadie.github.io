# THE ROAD
## What the Skin Remembers — Lore Document
### The Physical World Between Settlements

*This document covers what exists in the spaces between communities — the ruins, remnants, and evidence of the old world that characters move through on any significant journey. The intention is specificity: not the general fact of ruins but what they actually look like, what they're doing now, what they suggest about a world none of these people lived in.*

---

### THE HIGHWAYS

The interstates are the most alien things in the landscape.

The surface is cracked and heaved by root systems that have had five generations to work, but the grade is still there — the impossibly precise flat corridor cut through terrain that resists flatness, running in directions that make no sense unless you understand the logic of moving very large numbers of people very fast between cities that no longer exist in any recognizable form. The bridges still standing are the ones built to outlast civilization, and they did. Massive concrete spans over rivers, carrying nothing but weather. Guardrails bent at the places where something hit them, a century ago, the impact still legible in the metal.

The blue signs are the strangest part. They are still standing in places, still readable, because they were made to outlast ordinary abuse. *EXIT 47. INTERSTATE 81 NORTH. ROANOKE 24.* The distances are accurate to a city that is now a hill of rubble with trees growing through it. Naum has read one of these signs and understood every word of it and understood nothing about what it meant.

On-ramps form natural archways where the embankment has become a forest feature — you duck under the concrete to pass, and the underside is cool and damp and has been colonized by something that grows in the dark. The medians are forest. The lanes are parallel clearings, a green tunnel you can see down for miles if you stand in the middle, which nobody does for long because standing in the open on flat ground with clear sightlines in both directions is the kind of comfort nobody in this world has learned to trust.

Rest stops have become specific landmarks. The buildings collapsed decades ago but the concrete pads remain — flat ground in a landscape that is rarely flat — and travelers use them for exactly the purposes they were built for. The vending machine skeletons are rusted open, emptied, and have become storage containers in at least one community's territory. A community two days east of Holdfast has built a seasonal trading post on a rest stop pad using the original restroom building's walls, which were built from concrete block and have not fallen.

### THE VEHICLES

Passenger cars are almost gone. Mild steel has a lifespan measured in decades under typical weathering, and a hundred and twenty-five years of Appalachian rain and freeze-thaw has reduced most to rust-stain shapes in the vegetation — recognizable as cars only by their outline, which is still visible as a depression or a low mound of reddish earth. A few survived in conditions that protected them: under overhangs, in garages, in circumstances where the water didn't reach.

The large vehicles lasted longer. Semi-trucks, buses, military equipment — heavier gauge steel, built to take abuse. Some are still recognizable structures, though nothing inside them survived. They sit in positions that suggest the moment they stopped: a semi jackknifed across two lanes of I-40 with the trailer still attached, angled against the guardrail at thirty degrees, a hundred years of weather and a few decades of salvagers having stripped everything removable. The trailer is a shell. The cab is a shell. Together they form a specific kind of gateway — travelers have been passing under or around it for so long that a small path has worn on either side.

A school bus a day's walk south of the Writ's territory has been converted and abandoned and converted again. Someone turned it into a dwelling, with a wood stove vented through the roof and cloth hung over the windows. Then they left. Then someone took the stove and the cloth. Then a tree grew through the floor, because the floor rotted through, and the tree is now significant enough that it holds the bus frame up rather than the other way around. The bus and the tree have been in this relationship for a generation and both are doing fine.

Military convoys stopped in several places along the mountain routes and never started again. The equipment is too heavy to move without the machinery to move it, too specialized to repurpose easily. It has slowly become landscape — angular shapes under forest cover, the outlines still military but the substance mostly gone. One site, three hours east of the story's primary route, has a community living in the structures the convoy was carrying: prefab buildings, still structurally sound because they were designed for rapid deployment and built to survive being airdropped, surrounded by the rusting hulks of the vehicles that brought them.

### THE POWER LINE CORRIDORS

The steel lattice towers are still standing.

They were built to last, and they have. The wire is gone — too valuable to leave, stripped within a generation of the Collapse — but the towers march across ridgelines for miles in both directions, creating cleared corridors through the forest that have become their own ecosystem. Open ground in the Appalachian forest is rare. These corridors are edge habitat: the specific plants that need sun reach the corridor from both sides, the wildlife that prefers open ground uses the corridor as a highway, and travelers who know the terrain use them as navigation aids.

A tower is a known landmark. The communities in tower range know which one is which: the one with the broken crossarm, the one where someone painted a red mark at eye level three generations ago, the one that hums in certain wind conditions in a register that carries. Some towers have had platforms added at the top by communities that needed height — lookouts, signal posts, one that functions as the beginning of a fire warning system. The climb is not easy and the original ladder rungs are unreliable. People have fallen. The platform is still used.

### THE CELL TOWERS

Different from power line towers in what they suggest. They are everywhere — the Appalachian cellular network was extensive before the Collapse — and they are tall and they are structural steel and they have been dead for five generations and nobody alive knows what they were for.

The most common theory in communities that have them as landmarks is that they were some kind of signal fire infrastructure. The theory is not wrong in spirit. Communities have tested this by lighting fires at their bases and confirming that the towers make signal fires more visible from distance, which has reinforced the belief. Goins Hollow has two cell towers in their general range. One is used as a water tower now — a tank was mounted partway up by someone with ambition and adequate rope, and it has been repaired twice. The other has been painted, season by season, in colors that have no consistent pattern, by someone no one has identified. The painting started about thirty years ago. It continues.

### THE COAL COUNTRY

The Appalachian coal industry left enormous physical marks that will persist for centuries.

Slag heaps are hills that aren't hills — large mounds of coal processing waste that sit at the edges of former mine sites, vegetating at different rates depending on their chemistry. Some are now grassland, the grass a particular shade of stressed yellow-green that people in the region have learned to read. Some are still barren at their centers, forty years after the last processing, the soil chemistry inhospitable to most things. Communities have learned which slag heaps are safe to harvest from and which ones have water that doesn't behave right.

Mine entrances breathe. The temperature differential between a mine's interior and the outside air moves air through the opening in a direction that reverses with the seasons: in summer the mine breathes out (cool air releasing), in winter it breathes in (warm air entering). Communities that live near mine country know which entrances breathe which way and what it means. The open mines are navigable for short distances — useful for shelter, storage of temperature-sensitive goods, and occasionally for specific things people don't discuss openly with outsiders. The sealed mines are sealed for reasons, some of which are still valid.

The coal company towns are some of the most intact ruins in the region. The companies built to last: brick construction, laid by people who knew their work, on foundations poured deep. Some of these towns are inhabited — a community has moved into structures that were already there and that hold up because they were designed to hold up. The company store, the company housing, the company church. The company is long gone. The buildings remain. There is something that communities living in them work around: a quality of the space that suggests the buildings were built for a specific purpose that was not hospitality.

### THE DAMS

There are significant dams in the Appalachian region and they are some of the most consequential ruins.

The ones that still hold have created lakes. Communities on the shores of a pre-Collapse reservoir live next to a body of water that exists because of engineering decisions made a hundred and thirty years ago by people they have no access to. The dam itself — enormous concrete, spillways, the specific roar of controlled water through the gates — is a mystery structure that the local community has developed mythologies about. The roar when the spillways are open sounds like something alive. Communities have learned to keep the spillways clear because they've learned, empirically, what happens when debris builds up. They do not know why they're doing this in terms of hydrology. They know they're doing it because the last time someone didn't, something happened downstream.

A failed dam is a specific kind of wound place. The valley below it was flooded for generations and is now slowly recovering — the mud flats still hold the shapes of what was there before the flooding, visible at low water: the outline of a road, the footings of a building, the regularity of a field that was too geometric to be natural. At certain times of year, when the light is right, you can see what the valley was before the dam and before the flood, which is two different pasts simultaneously visible in the same place.

### THE TOURISM INFRASTRUCTURE

The Appalachian region before the Collapse was heavily touristed. The evidence is everywhere and consistently baffling.

Ski resorts are the strangest. At altitude, above the tree line, the lift infrastructure is still there: the towers, the cables, in some places the chairs themselves hanging frozen on the cable, swinging slightly in wind. The geometry of it is wrong — vertical structures at regular intervals across an open slope, connected by a line that serves no apparent function, the whole apparatus aimed at the top of a mountain for no reason anyone can reconstruct. Communities that live near them have stripped what was strippable. The towers remain because they're bolted to concrete footings poured too deep to extract practically. The chairs remain in places where the salvage was too difficult for the value.

The lodge at the base of one former resort is still structurally sound — enormous timber construction, built to impress, and impressive construction lasts. It's been used as emergency shelter, as a trading post for two seasons, as a community hall by a community that subsequently moved. It has a fireplace large enough to stand in, which is the detail most people mention first when they describe it.

The Blue Ridge Parkway is a ghost road. Fifty feet wide, built to scenic standards with gentle curves and careful grading, running for miles across ridgetops with views in both directions. The surface is broken but the grade is still walkable — arguably better than any other route at altitude. Communities use it as a highway in the literal original sense: a high road, a road along the ridge. The stone walls and guardrails are still largely intact. The overlook pads are still there, still flat, still facing the views the old world found worth stopping for. People stop at them now without knowing they were built for stopping. The view hasn't changed.

### THE PLANES

Someone is flying.

Not often, not reliably, not in anything that would have been called a plane before the Collapse. But someone in the region has reconstructed basic aviation and is using it, and the characters encounter the evidence of this without ever meeting the people responsible.

The first evidence is the landing strips: cleared fields, roughly maintained, with the grass cut low and the worst of the rocks moved. They appear at intervals that suggest a route — someone is going somewhere, regularly, and needs places to land between here and there. The communities adjacent to these fields have various explanations for them. None of the explanations are correct.

The second evidence is the sound — a specific mechanical sound that is not an animal and not the wind and not anything with a straightforward explanation, heard at altitude, moving in a direction. The party hears it once, at night, and in the morning there are no tracks.

The third evidence is a wreck.

On a hillside below a ridge that would have required crossing at altitude: an aircraft built from what was available. The frame is wood — ash or oak, bent and joined in ways that required both knowledge and patience. The covering is treated canvas over the frame, several types of canvas pieced together where the original material wasn't enough. There is an engine of some kind — the party cannot determine what it originally was, only that it has been modified significantly from whatever its original purpose. There is a seat. There is a control mechanism whose logic Tace reconstructs, partially, from the position of the components. There is a person who was in it when it came down, who is no longer there — the absence is recent, the blood is not fresh but is not ancient. Someone came and got them.

Tace crouches over it for a long time. She writes two pages. She does not close the entry.

The wreck suggests: there is somewhere worth flying to. There is a community with enough knowledge, ambition, and resources to build something like this. There is someone who got on it anyway. The story of where they were going and whether they made it before this one did is not a story the party ever learns.

### WHAT ELSE MOVES THROUGH

The party is not alone on the roads.

Wagon tracks on established routes — not their wagons, made recently, going somewhere with significant load judging by the depth of the ruts. A trade route that the characters cross twice without following. Something is moving between two points they never identify, in volume that suggests it matters.

Smoke on distant ridges: sometimes signal, sometimes cooking, sometimes a community the party never reaches. Three times on the journey to the Cold House the party sees smoke at a distance that implies a settlement of some size. None of these settlements are on any route they're taking. They exist, off to the side, living their own story.

A boat on the river, seen from the ridge above: a flat-bottomed thing, well-made, moving downstream with a pole. One person managing it. Moving too fast to have come from anywhere close. Gone around the bend before the party can get to the river.

Marks on trees along certain routes that Kess reads and Tace catalogues and Naum files away and Marn cannot find a reference for in anything the Keeper told him. They mean things. Some are directional. Some are warnings. Some are claims. Some are, in Kess's assessment, nothing to do with people at all. She does not elaborate.

### THE APPROACH TO THE COLD HOUSE

The approach to FEMA Cache Bravo-7 takes the party through terrain that prepares you, in retrospect, for what you find.

At lower elevation: the ruins of a small town, mostly brick, mostly still standing in outline. The main street is identifiable by the regularity of the building foundations. Something was here that people came to and then stopped coming to. The church is the last building with a roof. Nobody uses it.

At middle elevation: a section of road that has been maintained. Not well maintained, not recently, but intentionally maintained within living memory — the worst of the holes filled, the culverts cleared, the surface kept passable. Someone knew where this road went and wanted it to stay usable. That community or person is not present and has not been for some time. The maintenance tells you they planned to come back.

At high elevation: the solar panels on the south face of FlameTongue Mountain become visible before the structure does. They catch light in a way that reads as wrong — too regular, too extensive, not natural reflection. Then the concrete. Then the lights, at dusk, bleeding through the seams.

The Cold House earns its name before you're inside it. The metal construction holds the cold even in summer — the ambient temperature drops as you approach, the humidity changes, the sound changes. Something is preserved in there and has been kept preserved for a hundred years and the effort of that preservation is legible in the air.

Kess stands at the entrance for a long time before following.
