# THE WRIT
## What the Skin Remembers — Lore Document

*The tribe's name is the Writ. Their home ground is Holdfast. Outsiders sometimes call them the Recorder tribe, which the Writ consider a functional description rather than a name.*

---

### THE CENTRAL PREMISE

The Writ preserve memory by tattooing their history onto the bodies of a lineage of living historians. When a Recorder dies, their preserved skin is added to the longhouse archive. Memory is mortal, imperfect, chosen, and irreplaceable.

This is not considered a compromise. It is considered the truth of what memory actually is.

---

### THE ARCHIVE OF SKIN

The longhouse at the center of the tribe's home ground contains the archive. It is larger than outsiders expect.

The core of it is the preserved skins of every Recorder going back as far as the tradition holds. But the Recorder's skins are not the only ones. Members of the Writ carry their own stories tattooed onto their own bodies throughout their lives — significant events, witnesses, the shapes of what their years have made them. When they die, a portion of that skin, or in some cases the whole of it, is preserved and added to the archive. The Recorder's role includes the care of all of it: the Recorders' skins, the individual skins, the partial records of people who died young or whose skin was damaged before preservation.

The archive is organized but not neatly. There is an internal logic the current Recorder understands completely. The Scribe understands most of it. No one else does, which concerns the Recorder and which he does nothing about because the archive's organization is inside him and he does not know how to transfer it while he is still alive.

The archive reaches back five generations to firsthand accounts of the Collapse. The oldest skins are the most fragile and the most sacred. They contain testimony from people who saw the old world end. Some of what they recorded is interpretable. Some refers to things that no longer exist and cannot be explained. The tribe has stopped trying to explain those passages and preserves them as-is — evidence of something that once mattered even if the context is gone.

Children of the tribe grow up knowing the archive is there. They are not taken to see it casually. Access is earned and purposeful. The weight of it — the knowledge that their entire history is held in human skin, which rots and burns and tears — is something every member of the tribe carries.

This is understood as appropriate. Memory should have weight.

---

### THE RECORDER

There is one Recorder at a time.

When someone is selected as Recorder, they give up their name. They were Tomis Franklin. They are now the Recorder. The name goes into the archive with everything else they were before — a private tattoo placed at the beginning of their tenure, their old name recorded once and never spoken again. They become a vessel. The story of the tribe flows through them, not from them.

The stories on his skin are not his own. This is the central fact of the role and the central sacrifice of it. A Recorder begins their tenure with unmarked skin. The Scribe fills it, year by year, with the village's communal record — births, deaths, alliances, disasters, the names of the Written, the shape of seasons, the record of what the community witnessed together. He carries Holdfast's history in the only medium the tradition trusts: the living body, which cannot be separated from the weight of what it holds.

His skin is not yet full. The writing is small and dense — the Scribe learned early in her tenure that space is finite and stories are not. The progression tells its own story anyway: older passages faded and slightly blurred at the edges from years and washing, newer work sharp and dark, and the incremental difference between the two is a record of how long he has been the Recorder. He is not old exactly, but he is running out of room.

He is old enough. He moves carefully. He does not speak casually about what is on his skin because not all of it is for casual conversation.

**What a writer needs to know about the Recorder:**
- He listens without expression and without interruption. He has done this for decades and the stillness is genuine, not performed.
- He does not validate stories as they are told. He waits. If a story is worth tattooing, it will still be worth tattooing after he has had time to consider it.
- He knows that his archive is incomplete. He has always known this. Events are recorded without their consequences, because tribesmen return with what they witnessed and no tribesman can witness everything. This is the central limitation of his method and he has made peace with it, until Noan returns with the second half of an old story and forces him to reckon with it fresh.
- He considers the truth of his own tribe when exposed to outside accounts. This is not comfortable.

---

### THE SCRIBE

The Scribe performs the tattooing. She is the Recorder's hands.

The current Scribe was Filamina Franklin. She took the role when her husband Tomis was selected as Recorder — she learned to read and write specifically for this purpose, at a point in their lives when neither of them could have known he would be chosen. She had the instinct before the occasion. When the occasion came, she was ready.

She gave up her name alongside his. They are the Recorder and the Scribe, which is not a diminishment — it is what they chose to become together, and they chose it together.

**What a writer needs to know about the Scribe:**
- Her hands are extraordinary. Decades of fine tattooing work have made them precise beyond what seems possible. She does not tremble. She does not rush.
- She is the one who decides the physical composition of each tattoo — where it goes on the skin, how large, what surrounds it. The Recorder selects what is worth preserving. The Scribe determines how it lives in the archive. This is a creative act and she understands it as such.
- She loved Tomis Franklin before he was the Recorder. She loves the Recorder now. These are not the same thing and she holds both without confusion.
- When Noan returns with his story, it is the Scribe who bares the last remaining patch of unmarked skin on the Recorder's forearm. She has been waiting for this. She does not say so. She also chooses the small mark that goes onto Noan's own skin — the key to the story, the record that he was there. This is the last creative decision she makes in this sequence and she makes it without consultation.

### INDIVIDUAL SKIN AND THE COMMUNAL RECORD

Members of the Writ carry their own stories on their own bodies. This is not the same as the Recorder's function — the Recorder carries the village's communal history; individuals carry their own lives.

A person might be tattooed at the birth of a child, at the death of a parent, after a significant trade or conflict or season-that-was-different. The Scribe performs these as well, on request, at the appropriate time. There is no fixed schedule and no required set of events — the tradition holds that your skin should tell your life, and your life will tell you when something is worth keeping.

The archive preserves portions of these individual skins after death. Not all of them — preservation is skilled labor and the decision of what to keep is made by the Recorder, who has criteria the community respects without fully understanding. What goes into the archive becomes part of the communal record. What does not is gone.

**What this means in the opening scene:** The Scribe is working on the Recorder's forearm when Noan leaves. She is adding something to the communal record — a village event, a birth, a season's end, something the tradition requires be entered. This is ordinary work. She does not look up when Noan goes.

---

### THE COMING-OF-AGE TRADITION

When a young person of the tribe comes of age, they go out into the world with a single instruction: return with a true story.

Not a boast. Not a rumor. Not something witnessed by someone else and passed along. Something seen with their own eyes, lived in their own body, that can be made to mean something. The Recorder determines whether a returned story meets the standard. He has refused stories before — not cruelly, but clearly. The tradition requires honesty about what counts.

The young person goes out with no map, no assigned direction, no timeframe. They come back when they have something worth bringing. Some are gone for weeks. Some for years. A few do not come back, and this grief is held by the tribe without revision — they died finding a story, which is not considered a waste, though it is considered a loss.

When a story is accepted, it enters the communal record on the Recorder — placed there by the Scribe in front of the community. This is the moment of becoming Written: not the telling, but the entering. The story moves from the person who witnessed it into the body of the person who carries the village. It is, in some sense, given away. It still happened to you. But it belongs to Holdfast now.

The person who returns also receives a mark of their own — a single tattoo placed on their skin, small, chosen by the Scribe. This is not the story. It is a key to the story, a way of saying: this person was there; the full account is in the communal record. The two marks — one on the Recorder, one on the returned traveler — are considered together. One without the other is incomplete.

### THE WRITTEN AND THE UNWRITTEN

This is the central truth of the Writ's tradition, and it is not softened for children: **if you die before you are Written, you are gone.** Not remembered, not honored in the archive, not carried forward. The Unwritten leave no mark. The community will grieve them, but grief fades. The archive does not.

The Written live on. Their story is in the Recorder's skin. When the Recorder dies and is preserved and hung in the archive, their story moves with him — permanent, in order, legible to anyone the Recorder allows to read. The Written do not survive death. But something of them does, in a form the Writ consider more reliable than memory and more honest than monument.

**What this means in practice:**
- Children are Unwritten. This is not a stigma — it is simply their condition, temporary and expected.
- Young people who have gone out and not yet returned are Unwritten. Their families carry this.
- Adults who went out, came back, and were refused — whose stories did not meet the standard — are also Unwritten. This is rare and quietly devastating. They are full members of the community in every other way. The archive does not hold them.
- There are no second attempts at the tradition. You bring back a story or you don't. If you don't, you live your life in Holdfast as a full person, valued, contributing, and Unwritten. When you die, you are gone.

The Writ do not discuss this often. They do not need to. Everyone who grows up in Holdfast understands the weight of going out. The instruction — *return with a true story* — is simple because the stakes behind it are already known.

**What this means for Noan:** He leaves expecting a story of glory. The glory would have made him Written. He does not know yet that the story finding him is harder and truer than the one he planned, and that it will make him Written in a way that changes what the word means to him.

---

### THE NO-ONE NAME AND THE TRUE NAME

Before a person of the Writ is Written, they carry a placeholder name — what the community calls a No-One name. It is a name for a person who has not yet become themselves. It functions as an identifier, a childhood name, a way of being addressed before there is a self to address. It is not considered a real name and everyone understands this. The person who carries it understands it most.

When someone returns with a true story and is Written, the community names them from what they brought back. Not symbolically — the name comes directly out of the story. The Recorder and Scribe hear the account, select the tattooing, and the name emerges from what the person witnessed, what they carried, what the story made of them. The person does not choose it. It is given.

This is considered the first true thing they receive as an adult.

The No-One name does not disappear entirely. It lives in the private tattoo placed at the beginning of each Recorder's tenure — the old name recorded once before the new Recorder surrenders it. The Writ consider this appropriate. The No-One name is part of the story too. It is just not the name.

**What this means for Noan:** Noan is his No-One name. He has carried it for nearly eighteen years. He will not carry it back. What name his story gives him is not known until the Scribe finishes and the community speaks it. The story ends before the reader learns what it is — but the weight of its arrival is the final image.

**What this means for the Recorder and the Scribe:** Both gave up their true names — the names their stories earned them — when they took their roles. The Recorder was once Written. He had a true name. He surrendered it to become the vessel. The Scribe surrendered hers beside him. This is the particular sacrifice of their roles: not the No-One name, but the earned one.

---

### WHAT GETS RECORDED AND WHAT DOESN'T

The Recorder records events, not opinions. Witnessed actions, not interpretations. The archive is not a philosophy — it is a testimony.

This is its strength: the stories are true. Every tattooed account is the firsthand testimony of someone who was there.

This is its fatal limitation: events are recorded without their consequences. A conflict is recorded by the tribesman who witnessed the beginning of it. What that conflict set in motion — the alliances it broke, the children it displaced, the grudges it planted that bloomed a generation later — those require someone who was there for the second half. And most tribesmen come home before the second half.

The Recorder knows his archive contains many beginnings with no middles and no ends. He has made peace with this as the nature of the form. What Noan brings back — the second half of a story already in the archive — is something the tradition has never produced before.

---

### THE LONGHOUSE

The longhouse is built to last. The community has been maintaining and extending it for five generations — there are structural elements that are original construction alongside repairs and additions that are clearly newer. The archive room is the oldest part. It smells of the tanning compounds used to preserve the skins and something else underneath — something older, organic, that nobody has been able to fully eliminate and has stopped trying to.

The community gathers in the longhouse for decisions, for rituals, for the return of tribespeople from their quests. When a story is accepted, the whole community is there.

The Recorder and Scribe live adjacent to the longhouse, not in it. Their home is plain and well-made. There are no decorations. Everything in it has a purpose.
