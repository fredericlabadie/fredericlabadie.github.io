# THE WYRD
## What the Skin Remembers — Lore Document

---

### WHAT IT IS

The Wyrd is not a word the Recorder tribe uses, nor the True Keeper tribe, nor Kess's people. It is Tace's word — specifically, the word from her battered book on the scientific method, which uses it in a footnote about pre-scientific categories. She adopted it as a placeholder for her catalogue heading and it stuck. When she uses it with others, they understand her. This is the kind of word that survives because no better one exists.

The Wyrd is the anomalous. The wrong. The things that live at the edges of the observable world and do not behave the way observable things should. It is not evil. It is not supernatural, in Tace's accounting — she does not have a category for supernatural, only for *not yet explained.* It is present, patterned, and partially predictable. It has been present and patterned and partially predictable for as long as anyone in the Appalachian region can remember, which is longer than the Collapse.

Whether it is *more* present since the Collapse is an open question. The practical answer is: yes, clearly. The theoretical answer is more complicated — it may simply be more visible now that human activity no longer drowns it out.

---

### HOW DIFFERENT TRADITIONS UNDERSTAND IT

**The Recorder tribe** treats the Wyrd as something that happens to other people. This is not denial — it is a cultural disposition toward the measurable, the accountable, the storied. If a Wyrd encounter happens to one of their people, it gets recorded. If it doesn't get recorded, in some functional sense it didn't happen. This produces a tradition with very few Wyrd entries and therefore a people who don't think about it often. Naum inherits this disposition entirely.

**The True Keeper tribe** does not believe the Wyrd exists. This is the machine's active position, not a gap in its knowledge. The pre-Collapse datasets the True Keeper was trained on classified anomalous phenomena as folklore, as anxiety-management behavior in high-risk environments, as misidentified biology. The machine inherited this classification entirely. When asked about the Not Deer, it produces a behavioral profile for *Odocoileus virginianus* and notes Appalachian regional folklore patterns. When asked about the crossroads presence, it produces a survey of threshold folklore as pre-scientific psychological phenomenon. It does not say *I don't know.* It says *this is what this is.*

The machine never lies about the Wyrd. It does something harder to correct than lying: it explains the Wyrd away with the same unhedged authority it uses for everything else. A confabulation can be caught against a document. A classification can't. The True Keeper didn't get the Wyrd wrong. It deleted the Wyrd as a category of thing.

The tribe therefore approaches the Wyrd with the confidence of people who have been told it doesn't require a category — not aggressive, not malicious, but operating without the protocols that keep people whole. Marn has explanations for every encounter. The explanations are sourced. The sources are a machine trained on a world that had already decided not to see.

His crisis in the bunker is therefore doubled: the document shows the machine invented part of the founding narrative. But the entire journey has shown him something the machine erased from the present. Both failures are the same failure. He just couldn't see the shape of it until now.

**Goins Hollow** has practical knowledge accumulated over five generations of living adjacent to anomalous phenomena. This knowledge is oral, unwritten, sometimes internally inconsistent, and largely accurate because it has been tested against reality continuously for a hundred years. It covers behavioral patterns, danger signs, protocols, and which parts of the hollow to avoid during which seasons. It is not organized. It lives in the community the way all practical knowledge does — distributed, contextual, carried by the people who need it.

**Kess** understands the Wyrd as the invisible world made visible. For her it is not a separate category from the rest of reality but a layer of it that most people have lost the ability to perceive. She does not experience this as mystical. It is simply the case that the crossroads has someone at it, that certain trees are not only trees, that the little people have claims and courtesies that must be observed. She learned this the way she learned everything — growing up inside a tradition that carried it, refined across generations by the CRISPR gift that made her more sensitive to things most people can only half-perceive.

**Tace** understands the Wyrd as biology and territory not yet catalogued. She has been cataloguing it for years. Some patterns are clear. Some have been open for years. Kess is the first thing she has encountered that broke her model entirely — not because Kess disproves the biological framework, but because Kess is herself a data point the framework was not built to accommodate. A human being who is genuinely of the Wyrd rather than simply adjacent to it changes what the Wyrd is.

## THE CATALOGUE

*What follows draws on Tace's field journal, Goins Hollow oral tradition, and the Recorder tribe archive. Entries are listed in order of frequency of encounter, not danger.*

---

### THE NOT DEER

**Description.** At distance: a deer. At close range: wrong. The proportions are slightly off in ways that resist precise description — the legs too long or too short depending on the moment of observation, the neck held at an angle that suggests a different skeleton, the ears responding to sounds from directions that contain no sound. The eyes, if you meet them, are present in a way deer eyes are not. Something is home.

**Behavior.** The Not Deer do not behave as deer behave. They do not flee. They stand and assess. They have been observed to follow travelers at a fixed distance for hours without closing or retreating. They respond to being spoken to, though not in any consistent way — sometimes they leave, sometimes they approach, sometimes they simply stop moving entirely and wait. They are drawn to thresholds: the edges of clearings, the last treeline before open ground, doorways and ruins that were once doorways. They do not, in five generations of Goins Hollow record, appear to have caused direct harm. They appear to be watching.

**What Tace has established.** Not Deer travel in ranges. They are not solitary. There appear to be multiple populations in the high country, and their ranges shift seasonally. They are ranging further north than at any prior point in Tace's catalogue, which she does not have an explanation for yet. They respond to Kess differently than they respond to anyone else Tace has observed — they do not follow her. They yield to her. This is a new entry.

**What Goins Hollow knows.** Do not run. Do not meet the eyes for longer than a moment. If one stands in your path, speak to it directly and state your destination and purpose. Leave something at the place where you encountered it. Most people leave food. Tace leaves a mark in the journal and a small piece of cordage.

**What Kess knows.** They are keeping track. Of what, she has never been sure. She acknowledges them when she passes and moves on.

---

### THE CROSSROADS PRESENCE

**Description.** Not visible. Not precisely invisible — it is more accurate to say it is visible to some people and not to others, and Kess is one of the former. At crossroads, and particularly at crossroads where two meaningful paths meet (not simply a deer trail intersecting a game road, but a place where human choice has historically concentrated), there is a presence. It has no consistent form in Kess's tradition. It has obligations. It expects to be acknowledged.

**Behavior.** The crossroads presence responds to attention and to courtesy. It does not respond well to being ignored, which is the natural instinct of travelers who don't know it's there. The consequences of ignoring it are diffuse and hard to trace — things going wrong in the next stretch of travel, in the particular way of friction that accumulates rather than one large incident. Goins Hollow has logged enough of these accumulations to take the protocol seriously.

**The protocol.** Stop. Acknowledge the crossroads — Kess does this with a brief, low-voiced greeting that she learned from her grandmother and has modified slightly in ways she doesn't examine too carefully. Leave something. The clay bottle of whiskey is for this, though not all crossroads want whiskey and Kess knows the difference by feel. Move on without looking back for a count of twenty steps.

**What Tace has established.** The crossroads presence is real in the sense that failing to observe the protocol produces measurable negative outcomes and observing it does not. This is her entire entry. She has no further explanation and has held the heading open for three years.

**What Marn's tribe knows.** The True Keeper has records on "threshold folklore" as a pre-Collapse cultural phenomenon. It classifies crossroads practices as anxiety management behavior in high-risk travel situations. Marn finds, on this journey, that this classification does not account for the data.

---

### THINGS IN TREES AFTER DARK

**Description.** You hear them before you become aware that you're hearing them — a presence in the upper canopy that is not wind, not animal movement, not the settling of branches. A quality of attention directed downward. Whether they are visible if you look is a question that the relevant protocol forecloses.

**The rule.** Do not look up into trees after dark. This is Kess's rule, inherited from her tradition, and it is the only piece of Wyrd knowledge that Naum quietly absorbs on the journey without examining why. It is also Tace's rule, arrived at independently through field observation. She has an entry in her journal with a heading she has never closed: *Things in trees (after dark) — response to direct observation.* The entry contains two incident reports and then a gap. She stopped testing.

**What Goins Hollow knows.** They are there most nights above a certain elevation. They do not come down. The condition for them not coming down appears to be related to whether they are watched — observed or not, they stay above. The mechanism is unknown. The practical guidance is unambiguous.

**What Tace suspects.** The protocol feels less like appeasing something and more like not triggering a threat response. She has watched birds exhibit similar behavior around certain predators — the wrong kind of attention, at the wrong moment, changes the calculation. She does not look up. She files the instinct under *operationally validated, mechanistically unclear.*

**What Kess knows.** They have a name in her tradition, which she does not say aloud and does not share without being asked. She observes the protocol without discussion. She sleeps easily in spite of it, which Naum finds deeply unsettling until he stops finding it unsettling.

---

### THE WOUND PLACES

**Description.** Locations where the Wyrd is thick — where the boundary between the observable world and whatever lies adjacent to it has worn thin or been damaged. Not dangerous by default, but requiring attention. They are often, though not always, associated with places of concentrated pre-Collapse human activity: data centers, hospitals, places where large numbers of people died, places where something was contained and is now not contained.

**How you know you're in one.** Kess's chest tightens — not with a lie, not with a truth, but with something that has no category in her gift's usual vocabulary. Tace's catalogue shows consistently elevated anomalous activity in wound places. Animals avoid them without obvious cause. The light inside them behaves slightly differently — not dramatically, not in ways that photograph well, but in ways that the eye catches and the mind resists naming.

**The federal bunker.** The installation in the high country is a wound place. This is Tace's preliminary assessment before they enter, based on the behavioral patterns of the Not Deer in the surrounding terrain. The bunker itself is full of pristine pre-Collapse records, fluorescent light, and the specific quality of preserved human intention that she has been unable to categorize for years. Kess walks into it and does not come out looking the same.

**What Goins Hollow knows.** The hollows and the folds of the mountains have their own wound places, known by location and avoided in certain seasons. The knowledge of which places and which seasons is distributed — different community members hold different parts of it, which is by accident and also, somehow, by design.

---

### THE PALE ROAD

**Description.** A path that is there and not there. It appears in peripheral vision before it appears directly — most travelers who have encountered it describe first noticing it at the edge of their sightline, and then finding it directly underfoot. It does not appear on any map. It does not appear in the same place twice, or it appears in the same place only for those who know where to look.

**Behavior.** Travelers who follow the Pale Road describe it as shortening distances in ways that shouldn't be possible — arriving places too soon, or arriving at places they did not intend to reach. It does not appear to be malicious. It does not appear to be benevolent. It is more like a feature of the landscape that has its own logic and cares nothing for human destinations.

**Tace's entry.** *Observed at dawn on the eastern ridge. Visible for approximately forty seconds in peripheral vision before disappearing when I turned directly toward it. No tracks. No disturbance in undergrowth consistent with a path. Hypothesis: optical phenomenon related to ground fog and low-angle light. Returning to test.* She has returned four times. Her hypothesis is still open. She has stopped proposing alternative hypotheses.

**What Kess knows.** The Pale Road goes somewhere. Where it goes is not a question with a consistent answer. You can take it or not take it; it will not find you unless you are looking, and you should only be looking if you know what you need from the other end. She has not taken it. She knows someone who did.

---

### THE HAUNT

**Description.** Not a ghost in any traditional sense — not a visible figure, not a voice, not a dramatic presence. A residual. The specific quality of attention that very strong human intention leaves in a place after the human is gone. Hospitals have it. The places where people made decisions under extreme pressure have it. The Archive of Skin longhouse has it, in a form the Recorder tribe has lived with so long they cannot feel it anymore.

**Behavior.** The haunt does nothing. It is the most passive entry in the catalogue. It simply persists. The weight of accumulated intention sits in a location the way water sits in a depression — not moving, not threatening, just present. It affects the people who come into contact with it in ways that are diffuse and hard to separate from ordinary emotional response.

**What Tace is working out.** She is fairly certain the haunt is not anomalous in the way the Not Deer is anomalous. It is something else — something at the edge of ordinary human psychology. She does not have a word for the category it belongs to. She suspects it is the mechanism by which the Archive of Skin actually works: not just the tattoos themselves but the accumulated weight of all that witnessed story, sitting in the longhouse, pressing on everyone who enters.

**What the Recorder knows.** He has never been asked. He would not answer if asked. Some things in the archive are too close to examine.

## PRACTICAL RULES
*Distilled from Goins Hollow oral tradition and Tace's field observations*

1. **Do not run from the Not Deer.** State your destination. Leave something. Move on.

2. **Observe crossroads protocol.** Always. Even when you're in a hurry. Especially when you're in a hurry.

3. **Do not look up into trees after dark.** This rule has no exceptions that have ended well.

4. **In wound places, pay attention to what your body knows before your mind catches up.** The first response is usually correct. The explanation you construct afterward is usually wrong.

5. **The Pale Road, if you see it: you can follow it or not. If you follow it, know where you need to go before you step onto it. It will take you where you need to go, not where you want to go. These are sometimes the same thing.**

6. **Kess's method, whatever it looks like, works.** Marn learned this. Anyone traveling with Kess will learn this. The temptation to explain why it works is best resisted until after it has worked.

## OPEN QUESTIONS IN THE CATALOGUE
*From Tace's field journal — entries still open*

- What are the Not Deer tracking? Five years of observation. No conclusion.
- Why do they yield to Kess? New entry. No hypothesis yet.
- Things in trees (after dark) — response to direct observation. Entry not closed. Will not be testing further.
- What is the Pale Road's origin point? If it has one.
- Is the Wyrd increasing, or only becoming more visible? The distinction matters enormously and the data does not settle it.
- Is Kess the first person born this far into the Wyrd, or only the first one Tace has met?

*That last question keeps me up at night. I don't mean that metaphorically. I mean it is the last thing I write before I sleep and the first thing I think of in the morning. If she is not the first, where are the others? What happened to them?*

*— field journal, undated*
