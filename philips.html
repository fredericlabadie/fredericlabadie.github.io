What I bring that's less common: a working command of Sense-Making Methodology (Dervin) applied directly to analytics problems. Case 01 / Consumer electronics retailer · analytics architect · under NDA

A workaround broke. The transaction data confessed.

Quantitative signal → observation → Sense-Making questioning → product capability that shipped.

The role asks for someone who helps teams define the right questions. Most analytics practitioners skip this step and instrument based on assumed questions. Sense-Making is the structured approach to not doing that — and it's the approach that surfaces what the data alone cannot.
01
Quantitative signal
Anomaly inside an unrelated customer-lifecycle model: a meaningful cohort returning shortly after purchase to buy additional components. Nobody asked for the investigation.
02
Observation
Go to the floor. Watch how staff and customers actually interact. The data can't tell you where to look — observation finds what the data doesn't instrument.
03
SMM questioning
Neutral, SMM-structured questions with floor staff. Not "what went wrong?" — questions that let the diagnosis emerge from the people experiencing the friction.
04
Diagnosis & intervention
Root cause named by the people who knew it: a shadow IT tool built informally by staff to handle part compatibility had stopped working. The instrumentation response followed from the diagnosis, not the other way around.
What the data alone showed
A pattern. A meaningful cohort coming back shortly after purchase. Surfaced incidentally during customer-lifecycle modelling for post-purchase marketing — not from any dashboard anyone was watching.
What observation + SMM found
A shadow IT tool staff had built informally to guide customers through part-compatibility decisions had stopped working. The designed system never supported compatibility guidance. When the workaround broke, the gap became visible in transaction behaviour.
→
Outcome
The client subsequently built and deployed a full compatibility-checking configurator — now central to the business, with automatic part filtering, inventory integration, and a dedicated assembly service tier built around it. Root cause confirmed by the product that shipped.
Sense-Making / Dervin Observational research Neutral questioning Shadow IT detection Influence without authority

Case 03 / Major quick-service restaurant brand · analytics architect

Designing AI workflows that don't outsource the thinking.

A production alert-triage system on Claude API + Amplitude, with explicit decisions about where the AI assists and where human judgement is preserved.

The role asks for AI-enabled telemetry use cases. The interesting design problem in AI-assisted analytics isn't the model — it's the gates. A workflow that delegates diagnosis to AI removes the human judgement step that makes triage meaningful. The risk isn't automation; it's outsourced thinking.

AI-executed step Human judgement preserved

01
Suppress known issues
Reads alert channel history. Records monitors already posted today with peak values. Silences for the rest of the day unless escalated significantly.
02
Pull, dedupe, pre-screen
Last ~65 minutes from production and staging alert channels. Groups firings by monitor name. Drops without querying: overnight 0→near-zero spikes and misconfigured monitors.
03
Trend + error breakdown
Queries Amplitude for each surviving monitor's 7-day chart. Surfaces error-type breakdown. Known low-signal errors auto-downgraded.
04
Severity gate — with escalation override
Only Critical or Warning generate a post. Exception: a suppressed monitor hitting 2× its prior value overrides suppression and re-classifies as Critical. The gate cannot be bypassed by noise.
05
Post — plain-English summary
One structured message: grouped findings, chart links, error callouts, 2–3 next steps. Critical findings prefixed with a plain-English summary ("about 1 in 8 checkout attempts is failing") so the signal isn't buried in percentages.
Where human judgement is preserved
The system surfaces and ranks — it does not diagnose or decide. The plain-English summary exists because a previous critical checkout regression went unactioned because technical percentages obscured the signal. The human still acts. The AI makes the signal readable.
What cognitive surrender would look like
Auto-resolve, auto-assign, auto-deprioritise without a human gate. The escalation override exists specifically to prevent the suppression logic from becoming a way to silence a worsening issue with its own earlier post.

Before

What's on this page — and what isn't
What I bring: HIPAA exposure on protected data in a prior engagement — I've worked under the regime and understand the constraints in practice, not just from documentation. A Sense-Making methodology with roots in health-information research (Dervin's work was developed partly through health-information studies at Ohio State). What I don't bring: I have not worked inside a medical-device team and am not claiming hospital-floor experience or device-specific instrumentation history. SaMD-specific constraints, IEC 62366 / ISO 13485 / IEC 62304 / MDR compliance — I would learn these on the job, supported by colleagues who have lived them. Said plainly so you can weight it honestly.

Standard consumer analytics assumptions break down in clinical workflow. The instrumentation layer has to be designed around that, not retrofitted.

Layer What it measures Instrumentation approach Clinical-specific constraint
Completion
Did the clinician reach the end state? Task finished, form submitted, record saved.
Standard event tracking. Task start / task end. Time-on-task per step.
Completion has clinical meaning, not just UX meaning. A completed task may still represent a dangerous workflow.
Omission
Was a designed step skipped? Not an error — a deliberate or habitual bypass.
Bimodal time-to-completion distributions. Expected events that don't fire. Sequence-gap detection.
Omission may be rational (the step adds no clinical value) or a workaround signal. Must distinguish before intervening.
Workaround
Did the clinician complete the task outside the designed system? Shadow process, external tool, verbal handoff.
Cannot be instrumented directly. Surfaced through observational research and SMM neutral questioning.
Workarounds become load-bearing. When they break, the clinical impact appears — not the workaround itself.
Passive collection priority
Instrument for what can be observed without adding cognitive load to the clinical task. IEC62366 and consent constraints narrow the instrumentation surface — design for what's within it, not what would be ideal.
Omission is not failure
A skipped step is a research question, not a bug. Diagnose before intervening. SMM-structured questions surface whether the omission is intentional, habitual, or a signal of design failure.
Workaround detection
Shadow IT, verbal handoffs, and external tools are the telemetry gap. The only way to find them is to go where the work is done. Observation, not dashboard review.
Methodological grounding
Sense-Making Methodology was developed by Brenda Dervin partly through health-information research at Ohio State. The study guide I published includes a working question rewriter (Hugging Face Inference Providers via a small Vercel API) — an instrument for the exact "ask correctly" step that clinical interview design depends on.
